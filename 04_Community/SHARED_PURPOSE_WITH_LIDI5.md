# Shared Purpose with Lid I-5

## Purpose

This document summarizes the City of Seattle's Downtown **I-5 Lid Feasibility Study** and compares it with the emerging **Lid I-5 North** concept.

The Downtown study is not a direct template for Lid I-5 North. The geography, real estate market, neighborhood structure, freeway configuration, and likely public benefits are different. But the two projects share an important civic purpose: both ask whether Seattle can preserve the regional transportation function of I-5 while repairing the local damage created by the freeway trench.

The useful lesson is not that North Seattle should copy Downtown. The useful lesson is that the Downtown study gives us a tested framework for asking better questions about feasibility, public value, equity, funding, and long-term stewardship.

## Executive Summary

The Downtown Lid I-5 study and Lid I-5 North share the same basic premise:

**I-5 will continue to serve the region. The land above it can serve the community.**

Both projects respond to the same underlying condition: I-5 created a large physical and psychological divide through Seattle. Both projects aim to convert a barrier into a connection, add scarce public space, improve walking and biking, reduce freeway impacts, and use infrastructure investment to support a healthier civic landscape.

The projects differ in context.

The Downtown study examines a 0.8-mile segment from Madison Street to Denny Way. It sits in the middle of Seattle's densest employment, visitor, institutional, and high-rise real estate environment, where the lid could support anything from open space to major vertical development. The study found that a lid is technically feasible, but difficult and expensive. Cost estimates ranged from roughly $966 million for a lower-cost park lid to roughly $2.5 billion for a lid designed to maximize vertical development and private investment. Private development could help, but would not fully pay for construction and long-term maintenance.

Lid I-5 North is different. Its center of gravity is not a downtown high-rise market. It is a residential and neighborhood-connection project focused on Wallingford, the University District, Green Lake, Roosevelt, Ravenna, and nearby communities. The value proposition is likely to depend less on major air-rights development and more on daily reconnection: safer crossings, better bike and pedestrian routes, new public space, reduced noise and visual impacts, stronger local business districts, and improved access to transit, schools, parks, and the University District.

The shared mission is strong. The implementation logic will be different.

## Common Mission

### Reconnect places divided by I-5

Both projects treat I-5 as more than a transportation facility. It is also an urban condition that changed how neighborhoods relate to each other.

The Downtown study frames the lid as a way to reconnect communities across I-5, especially between the Downtown Retail Core, Denny Triangle/South Lake Union, Capitol Hill, and First Hill. It emphasizes that I-5 continues to function as an east-west barrier even in a highly walkable downtown environment.

Lid I-5 North makes the same argument in a more residential setting. Wallingford and the University District are very close physically, but the freeway makes them feel farther apart in daily life. Current crossings such as NE 45th and NE 50th are functional but uncomfortable, noisy, and car-dominated.

### Create land for public purpose

Both projects see a lid as a way to recover land where new land is otherwise difficult or expensive to assemble.

Downtown's potential uses include parks, civic space, housing, commercial development, cultural space, and high-density mixed-use development. Lid I-5 North's likely uses are more neighborhood-scaled: parks, paths, safer crossings, civic gathering space, green infrastructure, community facilities, and possibly housing or mixed-use development where feasible.

### Preserve regional mobility while improving local life

Neither project depends on eliminating I-5. Both assume the freeway remains important regional infrastructure. The shared proposition is that the city can maintain transportation function below while creating community value above.

That is a powerful frame because it avoids a false choice between regional mobility and local repair.

### Treat infrastructure as civic repair

Both projects belong to the same larger shift in transportation planning: moving from vehicle throughput alone toward access, health, safety, public space, equity, climate resilience, and neighborhood cohesion.

This is the most important shared purpose. A freeway lid is not just an engineering object. It is a public decision about what kind of city should exist around the infrastructure we inherited.

## Project Geography and Scale

### Downtown Lid I-5

The Downtown study area runs approximately 0.8 miles from Madison Street to Denny Way. The study divides the corridor into four analysis areas:

- Area 1: Madison Street to Seneca Street
- Area 2: Seneca Street to Pike Street
- Area 3: Pike Street to Olive Way
- Area 4: Olive Way to Denny Way

The Downtown freeway segment is very large and complex. The study reports that mainline I-5 ranges from about 160 to 218 feet wide, with an average freeway width of about 175 feet along the study segment. It also includes multiple ramps, bridges, existing lids, adjacent high-rise development, significant utilities, steep grades, and major downtown traffic volumes.

### Lid I-5 North

Lid I-5 North is still defining its exact boundaries, but its core geography is the freeway corridor between Wallingford and the University District, with connections to Green Lake, Roosevelt, Ravenna, and nearby residential areas.

Important reference points include:

- NE 45th Street
- NE 50th Street
- NE 40th Street
- NE 65th Street
- Ravenna Boulevard
- The Burke-Gilman Trail
- The University District light rail station area
- Wallingford's 45th Street commercial district
- The University of Washington and University District business core

The North project should probably follow the Downtown study's segment-based approach. Instead of asking whether one large lid is feasible, it should ask which segments are most feasible, which segments create the greatest public value, and where smaller crossing improvements may be more cost-effective than a full lid.

## Community Context

### Downtown: dense, regional, institutional, and economically intense

The Downtown study area sits at the meeting point of the Downtown Retail Core, Denny Triangle/South Lake Union, Capitol Hill, and First Hill. It is a regional employment center, visitor destination, institutional district, residential growth area, and cultural corridor.

The community questions are therefore broad and high-stakes:

- How can a lid reconnect dense urban districts?
- How can it add open space where population and job growth have outpaced public amenities?
- How can it support housing and commercial development without worsening displacement?
- How can it protect cultural spaces, small businesses, and communities already under pressure?
- How should benefits and burdens be distributed in one of the city's most valuable real estate markets?

Downtown's lid concept is partly about reconnection, but it is also about the future of downtown growth.

### North Seattle: residential reconnection and daily neighborhood life

Lid I-5 North has a different social center. It is about restoring everyday connections between neighborhoods that should feel close: Wallingford, the University District, Green Lake, Roosevelt, and Ravenna.

The community questions are more local and lived:

- How can people walk or bike between Wallingford and the University District without crossing a loud freeway edge?
- How can NE 45th and NE 50th become safer and more humane?
- How can the project support Wallingford's local business district and the University District's mixed-use core?
- How can the project serve students, renters, homeowners, families, seniors, local workers, and small businesses?
- How can the project add public space without accelerating displacement or making nearby housing less affordable?

Downtown's project is about reconnecting major urban districts. Lid I-5 North is about making adjacent neighborhoods feel adjacent again.

## Commercial and Real Estate Differences

### Downtown has a stronger vertical development market

The Downtown feasibility study tested programs that included major housing, commercial, hotel, and mixed-use development. The study found that vertical development could generate revenue, but not enough to cover capital and ongoing maintenance costs. The financial gap remains central even in Seattle's strongest real estate market.

That lesson matters for Lid I-5 North.

If Downtown cannot rely on private development to fully pay for a lid, North Seattle should not assume development revenue will solve the funding problem. North may have valuable adjacent land and strong market demand, especially near the University District light rail station and UW, but the likely development intensity is different from Downtown's high-rise office, hotel, and residential market.

### North's economic case may be more distributed

Lid I-5 North's economic value may come less from large development rights and more from accumulated neighborhood benefits:

- Better east-west foot traffic
- Stronger connections between Wallingford and the University District
- Improved access to light rail, UW, local businesses, parks, and trails
- Reduced freeway externalities near residential and commercial edges
- Higher quality public realm
- More desirable small-business corridors
- Possible housing or mixed-use opportunities on adjacent parcels

This does not make the project weaker. It means the business case must be framed differently. The North project should not try to pretend it is a second downtown. It should make the case for local public value, neighborhood resilience, and practical connectivity.

### Displacement risk is common, but different in form

The Downtown study emphasizes residential displacement, commercial displacement, cultural displacement, and the need for racial equity analysis. That applies to North Seattle too, but the affected communities differ.

In North Seattle, the most immediate concerns may include:

- Rent pressure near the University District
- Student housing affordability
- Small business retention on NE 45th, The Ave, and nearby corridors
- Property value increases near new public amenities
- Pressure on older apartment buildings and naturally occurring affordable housing
- Uneven distribution of benefits between homeowners, renters, students, and businesses

Lid I-5 North should adopt the Downtown study's equity discipline, but define it around North Seattle's actual housing, student, renter, business, and institutional context.

## Engineering and Feasibility Differences

### Downtown's freeway geometry is unusually complicated

The Downtown study found that lidding I-5 is technically feasible, but challenging because of topography, alignment, frequent on- and off-ramps, urban context, slope, parking, utilities, existing structures, and the load requirements associated with potential buildings.

Several lessons carry directly to North:

- Ramps are a major source of complexity.
- Slope can make public access and edge integration difficult.
- Supporting buildings is much more expensive than supporting open space.
- Parking on top of a lid can make development infeasible.
- Segment-by-segment analysis is essential.
- Fire, life safety, ventilation, seismic design, utilities, and WSDOT operations must be studied early.
- Rough order-of-magnitude costs are useful, but they are not a substitute for design-level engineering.

### North may have different physical opportunities

Lid I-5 North may have some advantages over Downtown, but these need to be tested rather than assumed.

Potential advantages:

- A more residential setting may support a lighter public-space-first program.
- Some lid segments may not need to carry high-rise development loads.
- The surrounding topography may create opportunities for better edge integration in certain locations.
- A smaller or phased lid could focus on the highest-value crossings rather than one continuous megaproject.
- The public benefit may be clearer at specific east-west desire lines such as NE 45th, NE 50th, and links to the Burke-Gilman Trail.

Potential challenges:

- The Ship Canal Bridge and nearby freeway geometry may create major constraints.
- NE 45th and NE 50th include freeway ramps and heavy vehicle movements.
- Pedestrian and bicycle safety problems may require surface-street redesign, not only a lid.
- Air quality and ventilation issues remain important.
- Utility, seismic, fire/life-safety, and WSDOT operational requirements will still be significant.
- The project may have less private development revenue than Downtown to offset costs.

The most important engineering lesson from Downtown is humility. The North project should begin with a corridor and segment feasibility study, not with a single preferred design.

## Transportation and Mobility

### Downtown

Downtown has high pedestrian, bicycle, transit, employment, and visitor activity. The Downtown study notes that I-5 still acts as an east-west barrier even though the surrounding neighborhoods are relatively walkable and transit-rich.

The study area includes major vehicle volumes, multiple ramps, congested bridges, planned bike facilities, and significant pedestrian desire lines, especially around Pike and Pine. It also has a complicated relationship with existing lids such as Freeway Park and the Washington State Convention Center.

### North Seattle

North Seattle's mobility problem is more neighborhood-scaled but no less real. Existing crossings at NE 45th and NE 50th serve important local movement, freeway access, transit access, business access, and regional travel. They are also uncomfortable for people walking and biking.

The North project should study:

- Pedestrian and bicycle volumes at NE 45th, NE 50th, NE 40th, NE 65th, Ravenna Boulevard, and Burke-Gilman crossings
- Collision history near freeway ramps and bridge approaches
- Transit access to the University District light rail station and bus corridors
- Connections between Wallingford's commercial district and the University District
- Whether small surface improvements can deliver near-term benefits while lid feasibility is studied
- How ramp operations affect possible lid geometry and public-space design

Downtown shows that a lid cannot be studied separately from the street network. North should adopt that same rule from the beginning.

## Funding, Governance, and Delivery

The Downtown study is especially useful because it does not pretend that technical feasibility equals project feasibility.

It highlights several practical questions that Lid I-5 North will also need to answer:

- Who sponsors the project?
- Who owns the lid?
- Who maintains it?
- Who programs it?
- Who pays for operations, repair, replacement, insurance, and long-term asset management?
- How are WSDOT, FHWA, SDOT, Seattle Parks, Sound Transit, UW, neighborhood groups, and private partners involved?
- What public benefits must be protected if private development contributes funding?
- What governance structure keeps the project accountable over decades?

Downtown's funding lesson is sobering but helpful: even in a strong real estate market, private development is not enough. Lid I-5 North should expect a blended funding strategy that could include federal reconnection grants, state transportation and climate funding, city capital funds, parks and open space funding, philanthropic support, institutional partnerships, value capture, and potentially district-based financing.

The North project should also make phasing central. A full corridor vision may be useful, but fundable early pieces will matter more.

## Lessons for Lid I-5 North

### 1. Use the Downtown framework, not the Downtown program

The Downtown study's strongest contribution is its method:

- Existing conditions
- Segment analysis
- Technical feasibility
- Public benefit goals
- Real estate and financial feasibility
- Governance and delivery
- Equity and displacement analysis
- Outreach and engagement
- Next-step planning

Lid I-5 North should use that structure but define its own program around North Seattle's actual needs.

### 2. Lead with connection, not development

Downtown needed to test major vertical development because the market and urban setting made that plausible. North should not let development logic drive the project too early.

For Lid I-5 North, the first test should be:

**Does this reconnect neighborhoods and improve daily life?**

Development may help in some locations, but it should serve the reconnection mission rather than replace it.

### 3. Study segments separately

Downtown divided its corridor into four areas and found that cost, complexity, and benefits vary by segment. North should do the same.

Possible North segments might include:

- NE 45th-focused crossing and public-space segment
- NE 50th-focused crossing and public-space segment
- Burke-Gilman/Ravenna connection segment
- University District light rail and business-district access segment
- Wallingford commercial-district connection segment

Those boundaries need technical work, but the principle is clear: the project should be assembled from feasible pieces.

### 4. Treat ramps as a defining constraint

Downtown found ramps to be one of the hardest design and cost issues. North should assume the same until proven otherwise, especially at NE 45th and NE 50th.

Any serious feasibility study should examine whether the project works with existing ramps, whether ramp changes are possible, and what tradeoffs those changes create for I-5 operations and the local street network.

### 5. Build an equity strategy early

Downtown's study makes clear that public investment can create displacement pressure. North should not wait until design is advanced to consider affordability, student housing, small business retention, renter impacts, and equitable access to benefits.

### 6. Make maintenance part of design

Freeway lids are not just capital projects. They are long-term public assets. Design decisions about planting, lighting, restrooms, programming, public safety, drainage, and materials create maintenance obligations.

Lid I-5 North should define maintainable public space as a core design requirement, not a later operations question.

### 7. Be honest about what a lid can and cannot solve

A lid can reconnect, create public space, improve comfort, reduce visual impacts, and possibly reduce noise. It cannot eliminate all freeway impacts. Air quality benefits require careful analysis. Cost will be high. Construction will be disruptive. Tradeoffs will be real.

Honesty will make the project stronger.

## Shared Purpose Statement

Downtown Lid I-5 and Lid I-5 North are different projects with a common civic purpose.

Downtown asks whether Seattle can use one of its most valuable and complicated urban corridors to create new public space, housing, commercial opportunity, and regional civic value.

Lid I-5 North asks whether Seattle can reconnect residential neighborhoods, restore everyday walking and biking connections, support local business districts, and create new public land where the freeway currently divides the city.

Both projects start from the same idea:

**The freeway connected the region. Now Seattle can reconnect the neighborhoods.**

The Downtown study proves that this idea is technically serious. It also shows that the work is expensive, complex, politically demanding, and dependent on deep partnership. For Lid I-5 North, that is not discouraging. It is clarifying.

The next step is to translate the shared purpose into a North Seattle feasibility framework: define the corridor, study the segments, document existing harms, test feasible lid and crossing options, build a realistic funding strategy, and invite the communities on both sides of I-5 into the work before the design hardens.
