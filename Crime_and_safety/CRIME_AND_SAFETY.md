# Crime and Safety Analysis
## Lid I-5 North Project
### Working Research Document

---

# Purpose

This document evaluates crime, public safety, and perceived safety considerations associated with the proposed Lid I-5 North project.

The intent is not to advocate for a predetermined conclusion. Rather, it is to identify evidence, mechanisms, case studies, and research questions that can help project participants understand potential safety outcomes.

The document should be updated as additional data, studies, and community feedback become available.

---

# Executive Summary

Interstate 5 functions not only as a transportation corridor but also as a significant physical and psychological barrier between neighborhoods.

Research from urban planning, environmental design, and transportation fields suggests that large transportation corridors can influence crime patterns, pedestrian activity, social interaction, and perceptions of safety.

The available evidence indicates:

- Freeway lids are not primarily crime-reduction projects.
- Successful lid projects often increase pedestrian activity and public presence.
- Increased activity can improve natural surveillance.
- Public perceptions of safety frequently improve after major public-space investments.
- Outcomes depend heavily on design, programming, maintenance, and surrounding land uses.
- Evidence regarding direct reductions in crime rates is mixed and should be interpreted cautiously.

The central question for Lid I-5 North is therefore:

> Can reconnecting neighborhoods and replacing isolated freeway edges with active public space improve actual and perceived safety?

## Observed Conditions

Based on community observations and preliminary crime-density mapping:

### Freeway Edge Effects

Observed conditions include:

- Lower pedestrian activity immediately adjacent to freeway infrastructure
- Underutilized spaces near ramps and underpasses
- Physical barriers to neighborhood movement
- Reduced visual connectivity across the corridor

### Perceived Safety Issues

Residents frequently cite:

- Poor lighting
- Isolation
- Noise
- Limited visibility
- Uncomfortable pedestrian crossings

These concerns affect behavior regardless of whether actual crime rates are elevated.

---

## Preliminary Crime Mapping Observations

Community-generated crime-density maps for the Wallingford and University District area suggest:

- Certain crime concentrations occur near freeway-adjacent areas.
- The freeway appears to function as a visible boundary in some spatial distributions.
- Crime patterns may differ significantly between neighborhoods on opposite sides of the freeway.

These observations should be treated as hypotheses until validated through formal statistical analysis.

# Conceptual Framework

## Actual Safety

Actual safety refers to measurable outcomes such as:

- Violent crime
- Property crime
- Calls for service
- Emergency response activity
- Traffic injuries

---

## Perceived Safety

Perceived safety refers to:

- Willingness to walk
- Willingness to bicycle
- Park usage
- Family usage
- Comfort crossing the freeway corridor

Perceived safety may improve even when crime statistics change little.

Both measures are important.

# Mechanisms That Could Improve Safety

## Increased Natural Surveillance

Urbanist Jane Jacobs famously described the value of "eyes on the street."

If a lid attracts:

- Walkers
- Cyclists
- Families
- Commuters
- Vendors

then public observation increases.

Potential effect:

- Greater informal monitoring
- Reduced opportunities for certain crimes

---

## Increased Pedestrian Activity

Areas with regular foot traffic often experience:

- Greater social accountability
- More witnesses
- Faster reporting of incidents

Potential effect:

- Reduced opportunity crimes

---

## Improved Visibility

Many freeway-adjacent spaces currently contain:

- Hidden corners
- Limited sightlines
- Underutilized spaces

A well-designed lid may:

- Improve sightlines
- Increase openness
- Improve visibility across neighborhoods

---

## Improved Lighting

Good lighting may:

- Increase comfort
- Increase usage
- Improve visibility

Lighting alone does not prevent crime but can improve perceived safety.

---

## Increased Community Ownership

Successful public spaces often create:

- Stronger community identity
- More regular users
- Greater stewardship

Potential effect:

- Improved maintenance
- Faster reporting of problems

---

## Active Programming

Programs such as:

- Markets
- Festivals
- Recreation
- Community events

can generate sustained activity.

Activity often matters more than physical design alone.

---

# Mechanisms That Could Increase Risk

## Crime Displacement

Crime may move rather than disappear.

Potential outcomes:

- Geographic displacement
- Temporal displacement
- Target displacement

Evaluation should examine nearby neighborhoods as well as the project area.

---

## Attraction Effects

Popular destinations attract:

- Visitors
- Vehicles
- Opportunities for theft

High activity can produce both benefits and challenges.

---

## Underused Spaces

Poorly programmed public spaces may become:

- Empty
- Difficult to supervise
- Vulnerable to misuse

Design quality alone is insufficient.

---

## Maintenance Failure

Poor maintenance can lead to:

- Broken infrastructure
- Graffiti accumulation
- Reduced usage

Research consistently identifies maintenance as a critical variable.

Questions:

- Does the space feel actively used?
- Is ownership apparent?
- Are boundaries clear?

## Activity Support

Questions:

- Will activity occur daily or only occasionally?

# Preliminary Findings

Current evidence supports several tentative conclusions.

### Supported

- Infrastructure barriers affect neighborhood connectivity.
- Public-space design influences perceived safety.
- Activity and programming matter.
- Maintenance matters.
- Connectivity influences pedestrian behavior.

### Not Yet Established

- That Lid I-5 North would reduce crime.
- The magnitude of any safety benefit.
- Whether benefits would be evenly distributed.
- Whether crime reductions would exceed displacement effects.

# Working Conclusion

The strongest current hypothesis is that Lid I-5 North should be viewed as a community-reconnection and public-space project rather than a crime-reduction project.

If successful, increased activity, improved visibility, stronger neighborhood integration, and higher-quality public space may contribute to improved safety outcomes. However, these benefits should be treated as potential outcomes to be tested rather than assumptions to be asserted.

Future project communications should emphasize evidence, transparency, and measurable outcomes.
