# PRELIMINARY_REAL_ESTATE_ANALYSIS
## Lid I-5 North study area: Latona Ave NE to Roosevelt Way NE, NE 43rd St to NE 52nd St
**Date:** June 22, 2026  
**Purpose:** Preliminary zoning / value scan for the blocks flanking I-5 between Wallingford and the U District, with emphasis on implications for Lid I-5 North.

---

# Executive Summary

## Core finding
The study area is not a single market. It is **two adjacent but materially different real estate environments separated by I-5**:

1. **East of I-5 (Roosevelt / U District side)** is a **high-intensity transit-oriented development district** with Seattle Mixed–University zoning, major height allowances, and a development logic tied to the U District station area and University Community Urban Center.
2. **West of I-5 (Latona / Wallingford side)** is still primarily a **low-rise residential market** with NR3 and LR3 zoning, lower entitlement value, and a finer-grained neighborhood fabric.

That matters because a lid does not simply “add park space.” It has the potential to **change the relationship between two very different land markets**. If the lid materially improves pedestrian continuity, noise/air conditions, perceived safety, and access between Wallingford and the U District, it should increase land value on both sides—but **especially on the west side**, where the urban-form and zoning context currently underutilize proximity to one of Seattle’s strongest mixed-use transit districts.

## Working implication for Lid I-5 North
For Lid I-5 North, the opportunity is **not** to assume uniform value creation across the study area. The opportunity is to create **differentiated value**:

- **East side / Roosevelt–45th corridor:** reinforce and accelerate existing TOD value.
- **West side / Latona–5th corridor:** create value by improving access, retail viability, and development optionality in places that are currently close to U District demand but not fully participating in it.
- **Freeway edge parcels near NE 45th and NE 50th:** these are the most strategic value-creation locations because they sit at the seam between the two markets and are the places most likely to benefit from a lid’s direct urban design effects.

The practical takeaway is that **real-estate strategy for the lid should focus on edge conditions, upzoning strategy, and retail-enabled pedestrian corridors**, not just on aggregate acreage or broad claims about “property values going up.”

# 1. Study Area and Framing

**Study area boundaries**
- **East:** Roosevelt Way NE  
- **South:** NE 43rd Street  
- **North:** NE 52nd Street  
- **West:** Latona Ave NE

This area straddles the I-5 trench between the western edge of the U District / Roosevelt corridor and the eastern edge of Wallingford / Latona. It contains:
- station-area mixed-use land near NE 45th and Roosevelt;
- lower-scale residential fabric west of I-5;
- a small set of strategic parcels and streets that could become development or commercial connectors if a lid materially changes access and perception.

---

# 2. Zoning Overview

## 2.1 East of I-5: U District / Roosevelt side
The eastern side of the study area is in Seattle’s **University Community Urban Center / U District growth framework** and includes **high-intensity Seattle Mixed–University (SM-U)** zoning near NE 45th and Roosevelt.

Known examples include:
- **1000 NE 45th St** — cited in a Sound Transit / LIHI TOD proposal as **SM-U 95-320 (M1)** with a **base FAR of 4.75** and **maximum residential FAR of 10.0**.
- **4100 Roosevelt Way NE** and related Roosevelt frontage sites — marketed under **SM-U 75-240 (M1)** or similar high-intensity U District station-area zoning.

### Planning meaning
This is not ordinary neighborhood commercial zoning. It is **station-area growth zoning** intended to support high-density housing, mixed use, and transit ridership. In value terms, east-side parcels are already priced with substantial entitlement value embedded in them.

## 2.2 West of I-5: Latona / Wallingford side
West of I-5, the study area is primarily a **lower-scale residential district**:
- **NR3** appears to be the dominant zoning on much of Latona and nearby single-family blocks.
- **LR3 (M)** and similar multifamily transition zoning appear closer to 5th Ave NE / freeway edge and some 45th-adjacent locations.

### Planning meaning
The west side is close to the U District, but it is **not zoned like the U District**. Its current value is driven mainly by:
- neighborhood residential demand,
- proximity to Wallingford and Green Lake/Tangletown amenities,
- school access and general Seattle housing market strength,
rather than by large-scale redevelopment entitlement.

## 2.3 East-west zoning asymmetry
This asymmetry is one of the most important findings for Lid I-5 North.

**East side value logic today:**  
Transit-oriented density, mixed-use redevelopment, land assembly potential, and high FAR.

**West side value logic today:**  
Primarily residential neighborhood value, with limited redevelopment capacity and weaker commercial intensity.

A lid that improves east-west connection can increase the value of west-side land **without requiring it to become U District high-rise land**. The more plausible path is **incremental value capture through selective rezoning, corridor intensification, and stronger retail frontage near lid portals**.

# 3. Urban Village / Special Classification Context

## 3.1 NE 45th / Roosevelt is part of a designated growth area
The area around **NE 45th and Roosevelt** falls within the **University Community Urban Center** / U District growth area and has also been shaped by the **NE 45th Street station-area planning framework**.

This matters because the east side is already part of a city-designated place for:
- increased housing capacity,
- greater employment density,
- transit-oriented mixed use,
- public-realm investment.

## 3.2 Why this matters for the lid
From a real-estate standpoint, Lid I-5 North is not trying to create value in a vacuum. It would be operating next to an area where the City has **already declared its intention to concentrate growth**. That is favorable, because the lid can be framed not as an isolated park project but as an **urban-center connective infrastructure project** that improves the functioning of a designated growth district.

# 4. Historic Resources and Constraints

The most obvious known historic resource inside or immediately adjacent to the study area is:

- **Latona School / John Stanford International Elementary**  
  4057 5th Ave NE  
  Built 1906; designated a **City of Seattle Landmark** in 1998.

In addition, the broader U District has a substantial inventory of older buildings and historic resources documented by Seattle’s historic resource surveys.

## Implication
Historic resources are unlikely to stop the lid, but they **can affect adjacent redevelopment strategy**, especially if:
- assembly opportunities involve older contributing buildings;
- a west-side connector concept relies on demolition or major alteration of early 20th-century structures;
- design review or landmark review adds time, cost, or political sensitivity.

For a value-capture strategy, historic constraints matter because they can **limit the parcels most easily monetized** or change the cost/feasibility of redevelopment.

# 6. Preliminary Commercial / Development-Site Value Scan

## 6.1 East-side development land is a different asset class
The east side of the study area includes land whose value is driven by **zoning and development yield**, not by the economics of detached houses.

### Key east-side reference points

#### 1000 NE 45th Street
- Sound Transit TOD site / LIHI affordable housing proposal.
- Approx. **17,815 sf site area**.
- Zoning cited as **SM-U 95-320 (M1)**.
- Proposed 12-story mass timber affordable housing project with ground-floor community/retail uses.

This is important not because it provides a clean market sale comp—it does not—but because it confirms the **development intensity the market and public sector believe is supportable at the east edge of the study area**.

#### 4100 Roosevelt Way NE
- Marketed as a **~7,500 sf parcel** with **SM-U 75-240 (M1)** zoning.
- Again, the value signal here is **entitlement**: this land trades as a high-rise mixed-use development opportunity, not as a neighborhood retail lot.

## 6.2 Why no clean east-side $/sf land conclusion yet
The current evidence base is good enough to say **east-side land is substantially more valuable on a redevelopment basis than west-side detached residential land**, but not yet good enough to publish a rigorous **$/land sf** or **$/buildable FAR** estimate for the study area.

To do that properly, the next step should be a targeted parcel-level land comp study with:
- current assessor values,
- closed land / teardown / redevelopment sales,
- allowed FAR and height,
- likely construction type,
- and residual land value under several development programs.

That is one of the most important follow-up tasks for the project.

# 7. What the east-west zoning difference means for Lid I-5 North

## 7.1 The lid’s value effect is likely to be asymmetric
The lid will probably not produce equal percentage value gains everywhere. The most likely pattern is:

### East side
**Modest to moderate incremental value gain**, because east-side parcels already benefit from:
- transit proximity,
- high zoning,
- U District growth momentum,
- existing commercial identity.

The lid would still matter by improving westward connectivity, frontage quality, and neighborhood image, but it would be **enhancing an already-strong market**.

### West side
**Potentially larger relative value gain**, because the lid can change the west side’s position in the market:
- closer functional connection to U District retail, jobs, and light rail;
- less “back side of the freeway” condition;
- better environment for neighborhood-serving commercial uses at lid portals;
- stronger case for selective upzones or mixed-use overlays.

## 7.2 Strategic geography for value creation
The most important subareas are likely:
1. **NE 45th / freeway edge parcels on both sides**
2. **NE 50th / freeway edge parcels on both sides**
3. **the 5th Ave NE corridor and blocks that could become west-side feeders to the lid**
4. **Roosevelt / 11th / 12th / 45th station-area frontage that could benefit from stronger west-side capture of pedestrian traffic**

In other words, the lid’s value logic is **corridor-based and node-based**, not simply area-wide.

# 8. Context from other projects

## 8.1 Klyde Warren Park, Dallas
Klyde Warren is the standard American example of a lid that changed perceptions of adjacency and stitched together two districts that had been functionally separated. The important lesson for Lid I-5 North is not that “a park makes nearby property values rise.” That is true but too generic. The more useful lesson is:

- the lid can create **premium frontage** where there was previously highway edge;
- the lid can make **previously marginal blocks feel central**;
- and the real value often comes from **adjacent redevelopment sites, improved pedestrian flows, and brand/identity change**, not from the park in isolation.

## 8.2 Freeway Park / Seattle and the cautionary side
Seattle’s own experience also shows the caution. A lid does not automatically create value if:
- access is confusing,
- edges are inactive,
- grade changes and visibility reduce comfort,
- adjacent land use does not reinforce the space.

For Lid I-5 North, this is critical. The real-estate value case depends heavily on **how the lid meets the street**, especially at 45th and 50th. If those edges do not support retail, daily activity, and legible pedestrian routes, the lid’s value uplift will be weaker than it could be.

## 8.3 TOD case studies more generally
The TOD literature and the project’s own financing research point in the same direction:
- land value rises most where **transport access, density, mixed use, and public-realm quality are bundled together**;
- value capture works best where there is **clear incremental value creation**, not merely a general hope that the district will improve;
- and development-based value capture is strongest when the public sector can align **zoning, infrastructure, and development timing**.

For Lid I-5 North, that means the lid should be treated as **one component of a larger station-area / urban-repair real-estate strategy**, not as a standalone amenity.

# 9. Washington State law and why “classical” land value capture is harder here

## 9.1 The basic issue
In many places, “classical” land value capture means some combination of:
- tax increment financing (TIF),
- special assessments,
- sale or lease of public land,
- density bonuses / negotiated exactions,
- joint development over public infrastructure,
- or direct public participation in redevelopment value.

Washington has historically been more constrained than some peer states. The state did **not** long have a broad, simple, traditional TIF tool like California, Colorado, or Texas. Instead, Washington built a patchwork of narrower tools:
- **Community Revitalization Financing** (chapter 39.89 RCW)
- **Local Infrastructure Financing Tool / LIFT** (chapter 39.102 RCW)
- **Local Revitalization Financing** (chapter 39.104 RCW)
- and, more recently, **Tax Increment Financing under chapter 39.114 RCW**

## 9.2 What has changed
Washington now has a more usable TIF framework under **chapter 39.114 RCW**, and 2026 legislation has made it more workable. But this still does **not** make Washington a frictionless land-value-capture state.

The constraints are practical as much as legal:
- increment areas must be carefully structured;
- public improvements must fit statutory definitions and planning requirements;
- the debt and tax-allocation structure is technical;
- the tool competes with other local priorities and taxing-district concerns;
- and the political/legal burden of proving the project’s value case is real.

## 9.3 What this means for Lid I-5 North
For this project, “land value capture” should not be treated as a single instrument. It should be treated as a **capital stack family**. Likely candidates include:

1. **Tax increment financing / increment area financing** tied to lid-adjacent public improvements and redevelopment areas.
2. **Development-based value capture** through upzones, density bonuses, or negotiated contributions tied to parcels that gain substantial development value from the lid.
3. **Public land / air-rights strategy** if the project can create buildable development pads or controlled development sites.
4. **Local improvement district / special assessment logic** if a legally and politically defensible benefit district can be defined.
5. **Joint development around transit or public land holdings** in partnership with Sound Transit, Seattle, or institutional landowners.
6. **Ground lease / long-term public ownership models** where public control of land can preserve upside rather than selling it off early.

# 10. Preliminary implications for value capture on this project

## 10.1 The strongest value-capture candidates are probably not the detached-house blocks
The west-side detached residential fabric may benefit from the lid, but it is unlikely to be the cleanest or most politically defensible place to extract value capture. The better targets are probably:

- **redevelopment parcels near NE 45th and NE 50th;**
- **freeway-edge multifamily or mixed-use transition sites;**
- **station-adjacent east-side parcels that gain from improved westward catchment and pedestrian volumes;**
- **any new buildable land, air rights, or development pads created directly by the lid project.**

## 10.2 Lid-created development land would be unusually valuable
If the lid geometry and engineering allow **buildable pads** rather than only park/open-space programming, those pads could be among the project’s most valuable assets. Why:
- they would have a direct relationship to new public space;
- they would sit on a newly created east-west connection;
- they could be planned from scratch for mixed-use activation;
- and they could be structured as a public asset rather than as a conventional private parcel.

This is the clearest place where a lid can convert urban repair into monetizable value.

## 10.3 West-side upzoning should be selective, not blanket
The zoning difference across I-5 suggests a blunt west-side rezoning would be a mistake. A better approach would be to study:
- **mixed-use or neighborhood commercial intensification at specific lid portals;**
- **multifamily expansion along 5th Ave NE / freeway-adjacent corridors;**
- **form-based or design-guided upzones that preserve neighborhood fabric away from the lid while allowing more intensity where access improves most.**

That is more likely to be politically viable and more likely to produce value exactly where the lid needs it.

# 11. Further study priorities

This is where the project should spend real analytical effort next. The preliminary scan is enough to identify the questions, not enough to close them.

## Priority 1 — Parcel-by-parcel entitlement and land value model
For every parcel within roughly 2–3 blocks of the likely lid portals:
- current zoning and overlays
- current assessed land and improvement value
- ownership
- historic-resource status
- likely redevelopment type under current zoning
- likely redevelopment type under one or two lid-supportive rezoning scenarios
- residual land value under each scenario

This is the single most important next step.

## Priority 2 — Buildable-pad / air-rights feasibility
The project needs an explicit answer to:
- **Can the lid support development pads, and where?**
- What floor-area program is structurally and financially plausible?
- Can pads be leased rather than sold?

If the answer is yes, the financing conversation changes materially.

## Priority 3 — Washington value-capture legal memo
Commission or prepare a project-specific legal/finance memo on:
- chapter 39.114 TIF applicability;
- whether an increment area could reasonably be drawn around the lid;
- interaction with Seattle taxing districts and school districts;
- LID / special-assessment feasibility;
- development-agreement / voluntary contribution mechanisms;
- treatment of public air rights and any Sound Transit or WSDOT interests.

## Priority 4 — West-side corridor strategy
Study a focused west-side strategy for:
- **5th Ave NE**
- **NE 45th portal blocks**
- **NE 50th portal blocks**
- potential small-scale commercial frontage and multifamily intensification

The question is not “should Wallingford become the U District?” The question is: **where can modest zoning change unlock enough value to help fund the lid while improving neighborhood function?**

## Priority 5 — Retail demand and business mix analysis
The project has already observed that the freeway edge near 45th/50th is relatively weak commercially. A lid only creates value if it supports a stronger local economy. That means testing:
- what retail square footage is realistic;
- whether neighborhood-serving food / services / small office / maker space are supportable;
- and whether the west side could absorb a more active mixed-use frontage if the freeway barrier is softened.

# 12. Conclusions

## 12.1 The real-estate story is promising, but it is not simple
The study area already contains valuable land. The east side is high-intensity TOD land; the west side is strong residential land. The lid’s opportunity is not to “create value from nothing.” It is to **change how value is distributed and what kinds of value become possible**.

## 12.2 The best argument for Lid I-5 North is not just amenity
The strongest real-estate argument is that the lid can:
- increase the functional catchment of the U District urban center,
- improve the development logic of freeway-edge parcels,
- support selective west-side intensification,
- and potentially create monetizable public development sites.

## 12.3 The biggest unresolved question is implementation, not theory
The value case is plausible. The hard part is turning it into a financeable, lawful, politically durable mechanism under Washington law. That requires:
- parcel-level economics,
- a serious legal strategy on value capture,
- and an urban-design concept that creates active, legible, development-supportive edges rather than a standalone park over the freeway.

If Lid I-5 North wants a credible business case, this preliminary real-estate scan points to a clear next move: **stop treating the corridor as one market, and start modeling it as a set of different value zones connected by a potential piece of urban infrastructure.**

# Appendix A — Notes on $/sf normalization

1. **Residential properties:**  
   $/sf is normalized using the square footage shown on Redfin / MLS pages. In some cases Redfin shows both “living area” and “calculated square footage.” Where possible, the figure used is the one associated with the sale listing or the price-per-square-foot displayed by the listing page.

2. **Commercial / development parcels:**  
   No rigorous east-side land $/sf normalization is presented here because the current evidence is not yet apples-to-apples. Development parcels in SM-U zones should ultimately be analyzed on **multiple bases**:
   - $ per land square foot
   - $ per buildable gross square foot
   - residual land value under a specific development program
   - and, where relevant, value of affordable-housing / public-private joint-development structures

3. **Use of this memo:**  
   This document is appropriate for project framing and strategy discussion. It is **not** a substitute for an appraisal, broker opinion of value, or a parcel-level financial feasibility study.
