# CAPITAL_STACK_AND_IMPLEMENTATION_PATHWAYS.md
## A practical funding and delivery memo for Lid I‑5 North
### How the project could actually get funded, who would need to do what, and where Washington law constrains the playbook

*Prepared for the Lid I‑5 North project*  
*June 2026*

---

# Executive Summary

This memo is the practical companion to **ECONOMICS_AND_FUNDING_v2.md**. Its purpose is not to restate why Lid I‑5 North is a good idea. It is to answer the harder question:

> **If the project moves from vision to implementation, what funding sources are actually plausible, what is the pitch for each one, what legal barriers matter, and what sequence of actions gives the project the best chance of success?**

The short answer is:

## Lid I‑5 North will almost certainly require a **layered capital stack** and a **phased implementation pathway**.
There is no single obvious payer. The project is too large to be a parks project, too public to be a conventional private development, and too urban and discretionary to be treated as a normal highway project.

The practical path is to assemble **multiple funding stories for multiple parts of the project**:

- **Federal “reconnecting communities” / multimodal / safety / environmental grants** for the lid structure, crossings, public realm, and barrier-repair elements.
- **State participation** because the project sits on the interstate and implicates WSDOT, plus possible state support tied to mobility, climate, and urban access.
- **City / regional capital funding** for parks, public realm, streets, and match requirements.
- **Philanthropy** for signature public-space, cultural, play, and community-serving components.
- **Development value** from buildable parcels, air rights, and ground lease revenue.
- **Potential local value-capture tools**—especially a Local Improvement District (LID) or tax increment financing (TIF)—but only as part of the stack, not as the whole answer.

The biggest strategic conclusion is this:

## Washington is a materially harder place to fund a lid than Texas or California.
Not because the project is weaker, but because the legal and fiscal tools are narrower.

Compared with successful cap projects elsewhere, Washington has several constraints:
- **No deep, mature, flexible TIF tradition** like Texas or many other states.
- **LID/special assessment tools are real, but politically delicate and legally tied to “special benefit.”**
- **Washington constitutional and statutory constraints make land-value-capture tools less elastic than many urban advocates assume.**
- **The project sits on an interstate facility controlled by WSDOT**, which makes design, cost, risk allocation, and timing more complicated than a city-only park or street project.

So the practical message is not “Washington can’t do this.” It is:

> **Washington can do this, but the funding plan has to be more disciplined, more coalition-driven, and more legally grounded than the examples people often point to from Dallas, Atlanta, or Philadelphia.**

This memo therefore does five things:

1. identifies the **most plausible funding sources** for Lid I‑5 North;
2. gives a **short elevator pitch** for each source;
3. flags the **legal, political, and technical barriers** for each source;
4. recommends a **capital-stack structure** for the project; and
5. lays out a **practical implementation sequence** from concept study to delivery.

# 1. First principles: how to think about the capital stack

## 1.1 Treat the project as four fundable components, not one monolith
Lid I‑5 North is easier to fund if it is broken into components that different funders can understand and legally support.

### Component A — Freeway barrier repair / mobility / safety
This includes:
- lid structure
- reconnection of streets and pedestrian/bike routes
- safety improvements
- barrier mitigation and access improvements

### Component B — Park / civic realm / environmental performance
This includes:
- open space
- plazas, landscaping, play, stormwater, trees, and public amenities
- public-health and climate co-benefits
- cultural or educational features

### Component C — Development-enabling infrastructure
This includes:
- parcel preparation
- utilities
- streets / loading / service / access for buildable parcels
- structural capacity or podium coordination where needed

### Component D — Commercial and operating infrastructure
This includes:
- small storefront buildout
- kiosk/market infrastructure
- event and programming infrastructure
- district management / activation setup

Different sources will pay for different components. That is normal. It is also the only realistic way to do this.

## 1.2 The lid should be framed as a **public project with development leverage**, not as a development project with some public benefits
This matters politically and legally.

The core public rationale is:
- freeway harm repair
- neighborhood reconnection
- safety and mobility
- public health / open space / climate resilience
- economic reconnection of two neighborhoods

Development value is important, but it should be positioned as:
> **“helping close the gap and sustain operations,” not “the reason the project exists.”**

That framing makes federal grants, state participation, and city partnership much more plausible.

# 2. Washington’s funding toolbox: what is different here

This section matters because it shapes the whole strategy.

## 2.1 Washington does not have the same easy TIF playbook as Texas
A lot of lid discussions casually reference **Klyde Warren Park** in Dallas. That is useful for inspiration but dangerous as a funding analogy.

Texas and many other states have a longer, more flexible tradition of tax increment financing and development districts. Washington’s TIF environment is newer, narrower, and more constrained. Washington authorized a modern local TIF framework in 2021, and the legislature has continued revising it, including 2026 changes to chapter 39.114 RCW. But it is still not the same as the broad, deeply normalized TIF toolkit used in some other states. citeturn0search1turn0search3turn0search11

### Practical consequence
Do **not** build the project’s primary narrative around “we’ll just do a TIF district like Dallas.” That will not survive contact with Washington law or politics.

## 2.2 Washington LIDs are real and powerful—but they are benefit-based, not magic
Washington cities can use **Local Improvement Districts**. Seattle has done so recently for the waterfront. A LID is a special assessment on property that receives a **special benefit** from the improvement. It is not a general tax. It must be legally tied to benefit and is politically sensitive because property owners pay directly. citeturn0search0turn0search8turn0search13

### Practical consequence
A LID is plausible for Lid I‑5 North **only if**:
- the benefit district is carefully drawn,
- the special-benefit case is defensible,
- major property owners are brought into the coalition early,
- and the lid is framed as materially improving access, value, and development potential for assessed parcels.

## 2.3 Interstate ownership matters
This is not a city-owned boulevard or rail spur. It is **I‑5**. That means WSDOT and likely FHWA requirements shape:
- design standards
- structural and safety review
- staging and construction risk
- allowable uses / clearances / ventilation / fire-life-safety
- schedule and cost

### Practical consequence
Even if the City becomes the civic champion, the project cannot be delivered as a pure city project. WSDOT partnership is not optional.

## 2.4 Washington’s legal environment makes “land value capture” more technical than advocates often admit
There are several distinct value-capture concepts that people lump together:
- LID / special assessments
- TIF / local tax increment financing
- negotiated development contributions
- air-rights or ground-lease revenue
- transportation benefit districts or related local taxes
- state-authorized revitalization financing programs

These are not interchangeable. They have different legal bases, different eligible uses, and different political risks. In Washington, that distinction matters more than in places with broader redevelopment authorities.

# 4. Federal source #1: Reconnecting Communities / Neighborhood Access style funding

## What it can plausibly pay for
This is the most obvious federal fit for the project’s core reconnection mission:
- lid structure associated with reconnecting communities
- pedestrian / bike reconnection
- planning, environmental review, and design
- capital construction that mitigates a highway barrier
- community benefits tied to reconnection and access

The federal **Reconnecting Communities Pilot** program explicitly exists to restore connectivity by removing, retrofitting, or mitigating highways and other transportation barriers. It can support both planning and capital construction. citeturn0search2turn0search5turn0search9turn0search10

## Elevator pitch
> **Lid I‑5 North is a textbook reconnecting-communities project.**  
> It repairs a freeway barrier between Wallingford and the University District, improves access to jobs, education, transit, parks, and neighborhood businesses, and mitigates long-term social and environmental damage caused by I‑5.

## Barriers / cautions
- Highly competitive nationally.
- Federal programs increasingly want strong equity, access, and implementation readiness.
- A lid that looks like a luxury park or speculative real-estate project will be a weaker fit than a lid framed as **mobility, safety, public health, and neighborhood reconnection**.
- The project needs a credible public sponsor and enough engineering maturity to be taken seriously.

## Practical next move
- Build a grant narrative around **barrier repair, multimodal access, public health, and economic reconnection**.
- Start assembling the data package: crossing deficits, access to transit and jobs, population served, environmental burdens, and neighborhood fragmentation evidence.
- Keep the commercial/development program in the background of the federal pitch, not at the center.

# 5. Federal source #2: RAISE or similar multimodal / urban infrastructure grants

## What it can plausibly pay for
RAISE-type grants are often useful for:
- multimodal access improvements
- complete streets and urban access projects
- public-space/transportation interface work
- planning and construction where transportation benefits are central

## Elevator pitch
> **This is not only a park lid. It is a multimodal access project that reconnects two dense urban neighborhoods, improves walking and biking across I‑5, and strengthens access to transit, jobs, and commerce.**

## Barriers / cautions
- RAISE is not a “lid program.” The transportation story has to be strong.
- The project needs to show measurable mobility, safety, and access outcomes.
- If the application overemphasizes park aesthetics or speculative development, it weakens the transportation case.

## Practical next move
- Package the lid with specific access improvements: 45th/50th reconnection, bike/ped safety, transit access, and measurable travel-time and network benefits.
- Keep a clear “transportation outcomes” section separate from the park and development story.

# 6. Federal source #3: other safety, climate, or environmental grants

## What it can plausibly pay for
Depending on the final design, the project may be able to pursue supplementary federal funds for:
- active transportation and safety
- urban forestry / heat mitigation / stormwater
- environmental justice and air-quality mitigation
- resilience or public-health-related improvements

These are unlikely to fund the full lid, but they may help fund **specific components** of the surface public realm.

## Elevator pitch
> **The lid converts a polluted, dangerous freeway edge into safe public space with tree canopy, stormwater treatment, safer walking and biking, and better access to daily needs.**

## Barriers / cautions
- These are usually component-level, not whole-project funding sources.
- The project must avoid double-counting or promising the same element to multiple funders without a clear cost allocation.

## Practical next move
- Build a costed menu of eligible “green and healthy public realm” elements that can be carved out for grant pursuit.

# 7. State source #1: WSDOT / legislative transportation participation

## What it can plausibly pay for
Because the project sits on I‑5, the state is not just a stakeholder; it is a necessary implementation partner. State funding could plausibly support:
- structural lid components
- interstate interface work
- safety and access improvements
- project development / engineering in partnership with WSDOT
- legislative appropriations if the project becomes a state priority

## Elevator pitch
> **This project repairs a state freeway’s damage to local mobility and neighborhood connectivity while improving access and safety around a major urban segment of I‑5. The state built the barrier; the state should be part of the repair.**

## Barriers / cautions
- WSDOT’s core mission is not urban placemaking. The project has to be framed in terms WSDOT and legislators can support: safety, infrastructure stewardship, mobility, and state-local partnership.
- State budgets are cyclical and politically contested.
- The state will likely resist becoming the sole or even dominant funder for park-like elements.

## Practical next move
- Seek an early **state-city project development partnership** rather than jumping straight to “fund the whole thing.”
- Ask for support first for **study, design coordination, and freeway interface analysis**, then build toward capital participation.

# 9. City source #1: Seattle parks / transportation / capital participation

## What it can plausibly pay for
City capital participation is almost certainly necessary for:
- local match for grants
- parks/public-space elements
- street and access improvements
- utility or public-realm integration

## Elevator pitch

# 10. City / local source #2: Local Improvement District (LID)

## What it can plausibly pay for
A Seattle LID could potentially fund a **portion** of lid costs if the project demonstrably confers special benefit on surrounding parcels. Seattle’s Waterfront LID shows the city is willing to use the tool for major public-realm investments when the benefit case is strong. citeturn0search0

## Elevator pitch
> **The lid materially improves access, visibility, development potential, and property value for parcels near the cap. A benefit district is a fair way for specially benefited owners to contribute to the public improvement that unlocks that value.**

## Barriers / cautions
This is one of the most important sections in the memo.

### Legal barrier
A LID is not a general tax. It requires a **special benefit** finding tied to affected properties. That means:
- the district boundary matters enormously;
- assessment methodology must be defensible;
- property owners can and will challenge perceived overreach.

### Political barrier
A LID is hard if large property owners oppose it. It is much easier if a few major beneficiaries are early champions.

### Practical barrier
LID proceeds are unlikely to fund the whole project. This is a gap-closing tool, not the full capital stack.

## Practical next move
- Commission an **early benefit-area screening memo** before publicly treating a LID as a major funding source.
- Quietly test the concept with major institutional and property stakeholders near the corridor.
- Assume the LID is **optional upside**, not the keystone, until that work is done.

# 11. City / local source #3: Local Tax Increment Financing (TIF / LTIF)

## What it can plausibly pay for
Washington’s TIF statute (chapter 39.114 RCW) allows local governments to establish increment areas and use tax allocation revenues for public improvement costs, subject to statutory requirements and limitations. The state treasurer reviews project analyses, and the Department of Revenue has published updates on the program, including 2026 legislative changes. citeturn0search1turn0search3turn0search11

If Seattle can lawfully structure an increment area around lid-related improvements and development parcels, TIF could help fund:
- public improvements in the increment area
- debt service for certain eligible capital costs
- potentially a portion of development-enabling infrastructure

## Elevator pitch
> **The lid creates long-term taxable value and should be able to recycle a portion of that incremental value back into the public improvements that made it possible.**

## Barriers / cautions
This tool is promising but easy to oversell.

### Why Washington is different
- Washington’s TIF framework is newer and more constrained than classic redevelopment TIF in other states.
- There are assessed-value limits and statutory conditions on increment areas. 2025/2026 legislative changes matter, and the rules are not “set it and forget it.” citeturn0search1turn0search11
- Public improvements and timing must fit statutory requirements.
- TIF may not be large enough, fast enough, or flexible enough to serve as the project’s sole backbone.

### Seattle-specific caution
Seattle’s tax base and increment-area politics are complicated. A TIF district around the lid would need serious legal and financial analysis before it becomes a public talking point.

## Practical next move
- Ask counsel and a public-finance advisor for a **go/no-go TIF memo** specific to the lid geography.
- Treat TIF as **potentially important but still speculative** until that memo is in hand.

## What it can plausibly pay for
A citywide or subarea transportation funding measure could potentially support:
- debt service for transportation-oriented elements
- local match for grants
- access and crossing improvements associated with the lid

This is not a dedicated lid tool, but it may be relevant if Seattle adopts broader transportation revenue packages.

## Barriers / cautions
- Competes with many other transportation needs.
- Hard to dedicate unless the project is a major city priority.
- Politically dependent on broader city budget and ballot dynamics.

## Practical next move

# 13. Private / quasi-private source #1: ground lease and air-rights development

## What it can plausibly pay for
This is one of the strongest project-specific tools available.

If the lid creates buildable parcels and those parcels are publicly controlled, the project can potentially use:
- upfront lease payments
- recurring ground lease income
- developer participation in shared infrastructure
- negotiated contributions tied to development rights

This can help pay for:
- development-enabling infrastructure
- some share of public-space improvements
- operations or reserves if structured well

## Elevator pitch
> **The lid creates high-value urban land at the seam of two strong neighborhoods. That land should help pay for the public infrastructure that created it, while remaining under public control through long-term ground lease where feasible.**

## Barriers / cautions
- Development value is real, but it is not infinite.
- The market may not support aggressive assumptions if structural costs, noise, or design constraints reduce parcel efficiency.
- Selling off all value early may undermine long-term public control and operating revenue.

## Practical next move
- Prefer **ground lease analysis** over a simple “sell the parcels” mindset.
- Commission a parcel-by-parcel highest-and-best-use and residual-land-value analysis once a lid geometry exists.

# 14. Private / quasi-private source #2: philanthropic capital

## What it can plausibly pay for
Philanthropy is most plausible for:
- signature park features
- family and youth amenities
- cultural and public-art components
- community facilities
- programming endowment
- naming opportunities tied to public space

Projects like **Klyde Warren Park** and **Presidio Tunnel Tops** show philanthropy can be substantial when the public-space vision is strong, even though the institutional contexts differ. Klyde Warren’s funding stack included a major private fundraising component, while Seattle’s own waterfront also paired public funding with philanthropy and a LID. citeturn0search0turn0search73

## Elevator pitch
> **This is a once-in-a-generation civic-repair project that creates new public space, reconnects neighborhoods, and gives Seattle a signature place for families, culture, and everyday life.**

## Barriers / cautions
- Philanthropy usually wants something visible and emotionally legible. It is easier to raise money for a playground, pavilion, or cultural feature than for structural concrete.
- Philanthropy should be treated as a **complement**, not the primary backbone, unless a very unusual lead donor emerges.
- Donor influence and naming issues should be anticipated.

## Practical next move
- Develop a philanthropic menu of **fundable, visible public-space elements** rather than asking donors to “fund the lid.”
- Use philanthropy to improve quality and activation, not to backfill core structural obligations that ought to be public.

# 15. Private / quasi-private source #3: commercial and operating revenue

## What it can plausibly pay for
This is not a core capital source, but it matters for long-term sustainability:
- storefront rent
- kiosk / market fees
- concessions
- event revenue
- sponsorships or programming partnerships

## Elevator pitch
> **If the lid becomes a real neighborhood seam rather than a pass-through park, its commercial edges and programming can help support maintenance, activation, and public use over time.**

## Barriers / cautions
- Do not assume operating revenue will be large enough to carry debt service on the lid itself.
- Commercial success depends heavily on design, programming, and comfort at the freeway edge.
- This is more useful for **operations and activation** than for primary capital.

## Practical next move
- Build a conservative operating pro forma with “high / medium / low” revenue assumptions. Use it to support stewardship planning, not heroic capital assumptions.

# 16. What probably should **not** be the primary plan

## 16.1 “The lid will pay for itself through development”
That is not a credible base case in Seattle today. Development value should be part of the stack, not the sole answer.

## 16.2 “A TIF district will solve the funding gap”
Maybe it helps. It is not safe to assume more than that until Seattle-specific legal and fiscal analysis is done.

## 16.3 “Philanthropy will fund the whole park”
Possible in theory, but too speculative to anchor the plan.

## 16.4 “WSDOT will pay because it’s their freeway”
WSDOT is necessary, but it is not going to write a blank check for a city-building project without a much broader stack.

This is the practical recommendation.

## Core stack (most realistic)
### Bucket 1 — Federal capital grants
Use these for:
- barrier repair
- lid structure associated with reconnection
- multimodal access and safety
- planning / design / environmental work

### Bucket 2 — State / WSDOT participation
Use this for:
- interstate interface costs

- state-share transportation and access benefits

### Bucket 3 — City capital and local match
Use this for:
- match requirements
- parks/public-realm components
- local access improvements
- early project development and staffing

### Bucket 4 — Development value
Use this for:
- development-enabling infrastructure
- partial capital gap closing
- possibly dedicated operations reserve or maintenance support

### Bucket 5 — Philanthropy
Use this for:
- signature public-space elements
- play, culture, education, public art
- activation endowment or early programming

### Bucket 6 — Optional local value capture
Use LID and/or TIF only if:
- the legal analysis is favorable,
- the benefit case is defensible,
- and stakeholder politics are workable.

Treat this as upside, not as the base assumption.

# 18. Recommended implementation pathway

This is the most important part of the memo.

The project should not try to solve every funding question at once. It should move through **five practical stages**.

---

# Stage 1 — Prove the project and define the geometry (0–18 months)

## Objective
Get from “interesting concept” to “credible project with a defined lid footprint, rough cost envelope, and clear public sponsor.”

## Deliverables
1. **Conceptual engineering and geometry**
   - what span(s) are physically plausible?
   - where are buildable parcels?
   - where do 45th and 50th commercial nodes sit?
   - what are the major structural constraints?

2. **Order-of-magnitude cost estimate**
   - deck only
   - deck + park
   - deck + park + development-enabling infrastructure
   - phased options

3. **Benefits case**
   - mobility, safety, health, open space, economic reconnection
   - strong visual story of current barrier conditions and lid benefits

4. **Sponsorship map**
   - who is the lead public sponsor?
   - who is the state partner?
   - what role do the City, WSDOT, and community advisory body each play?

## Funding purpose of Stage 1
Stage 1 is about making the project fundable. It is not yet about building it.

---

# Stage 2 — Build the grant-ready and legislation-ready package (12–30 months)

## Objective
Create the package needed to pursue federal and state money.

## Deliverables
1. **Grant narrative package**
   - reconnecting communities pitch
   - multimodal/safety pitch
   - climate/public-health co-benefits
   - benefit-cost and equity framing

2. **Environmental and predevelopment roadmap**
   - NEPA/SEPA path
   - permitting and agency approvals
   - risk register

3. **Early legal/finance memos**
   - LID feasibility memo
   - TIF feasibility memo
   - parcel ownership / air-rights memo
   - governance options memo

4. **Political coalition plan**
   - Seattle delegation / state legislators
   - WSDOT leadership
   - nearby institutions and major landowners
   - neighborhood coalition and philanthropy prospects

## Funding purpose of Stage 2
This is where the project becomes eligible for serious outside money.

---

# Stage 3 — Lock the public core funding (24–48 months)

## Objective
Secure the public backbone of the project before overcommitting to development or philanthropy.

## Priority sequence
### 1. Federal grants
Pursue the lid as a reconnecting communities / multimodal / barrier repair project.

### 2. State partnership and legislative support
Use federal momentum to push for state match or direct participation.

### 3. City match and project-development commitment
Secure city capital support and formal project governance.

## What should be true before leaving Stage 3
- at least one major public funding source is secured or highly probable;
- WSDOT’s role is defined;
- the City has committed real capital or match;
- the project has a realistic path to environmental clearance and design.

---

# Stage 4 — Add development value, philanthropy, and optional value capture (36–60 months)

## Objective
Close the remaining gap and improve project quality.

## Workstreams
1. **Parcel valuation and ground lease strategy**
2. **Developer interest / procurement sounding**
3. **Philanthropic campaign for signature public-space elements**
4. **LID/TIF decision point** based on actual legal and political feasibility
5. **commercial activation and operating plan**

## Important discipline
Do not let Stage 4 drive the project before Stage 3. If development value becomes the dominant driver too early, the project may lose political legitimacy and federal/state competitiveness.

---

# Stage 5 — Delivery and long-term operations (post-award / pre-construction through opening)

## Objective
Make sure the lid opens with a viable operating model, not just a ribbon cutting.

## Must-have decisions
- Who maintains the park and public realm?
- Who manages commercial tenants and kiosks?
- Who programs events?
- How are lease revenue, sponsorships, and operating subsidies allocated?
- What reserve exists for major lifecycle maintenance?

## Strong recommendation
Create an operating entity or partnership before opening. Options include:
- conservancy
- nonprofit operating partner
- city-led model with dedicated district manager
- hybrid public-development / park-management structure

# 21. Bottom line

The funding path for Lid I‑5 North is difficult but legible.

The project should **not** chase a single silver bullet. It should build a disciplined stack:

- federal barrier-repair and multimodal money for the public core;
- state/WSDOT partnership because it is I‑5;
- city capital and match because this is a Seattle civic project;
- development value because the lid creates scarce urban land;
- philanthropy for the visible public realm;
- and LID/TIF only if the legal and political groundwork proves they are viable.

Washington is harder than Texas or California because the legal tools are narrower and the interstate/state role is more constraining. That is not fatal. It just means the project needs to be more explicit about what each funding source is buying, and more disciplined about sequencing.

The practical goal is not to “find the money.” It is to make the project **fundable** by turning a compelling vision into a package of components that different funders, agencies, and partners can each say yes to.
