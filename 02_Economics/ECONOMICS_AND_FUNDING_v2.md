# ECONOMICS_AND_FUNDING_v2.md
## Economics, Funding, and Business Case for Lid I‑5 North
### Reconnecting Wallingford and the University District across I‑5

*Prepared for the Lid I‑5 North project*  
*Version 2 — June 2026*

---

# Executive Summary

Lid I‑5 North should be understood as a **hybrid public-space, transportation-repair, and development project** rather than as a park alone. Its economic logic rests on four related propositions:

1. **Reconnection creates public value.**  
   The lid would repair a freeway barrier between the **University District** and **Wallingford**, improving walkability, safety, neighborhood cohesion, environmental quality, and access to open space.

2. **Reconnection can also create economic value.**  
   By turning the freeway edge into usable land, the project can create new development sites, new commercial frontage, and new opportunities for neighborhood-serving businesses and public destinations.

3. **A viable capital stack will almost certainly be blended.**  
   No single source is likely to fund a project of this scale. The most plausible strategy is a **layered stack** of federal and state grants, city and regional contributions, philanthropy, and land-value capture tied to development parcels or district-wide benefits.

4. **Business development is part of the funding case, but not the whole case.**  
   The project should not be justified only as real-estate arbitrage. Its strongest case is a broader one: **civic repair plus climate, health, mobility, open space, and economic development**. Development and commercial value should help pay for the project, not define it entirely.

This v2 document combines the earlier funding/business-case structure with a new **commercial development strategy** informed by Seattle case studies from **Othello**, **Mount Baker**, and **Columbia City**.

# 1. Project economics in one sentence

**Lid I‑5 North is financially most plausible if it is treated as a multi-benefit public project with a mixed capital stack, while using a limited amount of development value, commercial activation, and long-term operating revenue to reduce the burden on any one public source.**

# 2. Working project program and value structure

The current project concept has generally been framed around a lid between **NE 45th Street and NE 50th Street** spanning the I‑5 trench between the **University District**, **Wallingford**, and the Ravenna edge.

A useful working program assumption for economic planning has been:

- **~22 acres total lid area**
- **~6 acres buildable development parcels**
- **~3 acres roads / circulation / access**
- **~13 acres open space / park / civic realm**

Those numbers may change as engineering and urban design evolve, but they are useful for understanding the business case.

## Why the mix matters
This is not a pure park lid and not a pure development lid. It is best understood as a **park + mixed-use district hybrid**:
- the **park/open-space component** strengthens the public and political case and supports philanthropy, public grants, and environmental/health funding;
- the **development component** creates opportunities for land value capture, air-rights revenue, long-term ground lease income, and business activation;
- the **commercial/civic seam component** helps reconnect the U‑District and Wallingford economies and improves the long-term operating case.

---

# 3. Major categories of economic value

The project can create value in at least six different ways.

## 3.1 Public-health and quality-of-life value
- reduced barrier effect and better neighborhood connectivity
- better walking and biking conditions
- new park and recreation access
- potential reduction in some exposure to noise / localized pollution depending on design
- improved mental and social well-being through reconnection and public space

These benefits do not directly pay debt service, but they are central to grant competitiveness and political legitimacy.

## 3.2 Property and development value
A lid can convert low-value edge conditions into high-value frontage and development parcels. In a strong urban market such as the U‑District / Wallingford seam, this can create:
- air-rights or development-pad value
- long-term ground lease income
- property tax uplift (where legally capturable)
- stronger adjacent property values and redevelopment interest

## 3.3 Commercial and small-business value
The project can create:
- new storefront opportunities
- new food / beverage / neighborhood-service space
- programmed event and market space
- stronger east–west foot traffic between the U‑District and Wallingford

This matters not only for tax base, but for district vitality and public support.

## 3.4 Transportation and access value
The project can improve:
- local crossing convenience
- multimodal access
- network resilience
- perceived and actual travel time on foot or by bike between neighborhoods

These are transportation benefits that strengthen the case for state and federal funding.

## 3.5 Environmental and climate value
Potential value streams include:
- urban tree canopy and heat-island reduction
- stormwater and green infrastructure benefits
- mode-shift support through better walking and biking conditions
- environmental justice and public-health framing in a historically burdened corridor

## 3.6 Civic and symbolic value
Projects of this type can become signature civic spaces, strengthening neighborhood identity, philanthropic interest, and long-term public support. This is not fluff. It affects fundraising, coalition durability, and the willingness of agencies to participate.

# 4. Cost structure: what the project is likely paying for

A freeway lid project is expensive because it combines heavy civil infrastructure with public-space construction and, potentially, development-ready urban district work.

The cost categories typically include:

## 4.1 Structural lid and freeway interface
- deck structure and support systems
- retaining-wall / abutment / interface work
- ventilation, fire-life-safety, drainage, and utility coordination
- traffic management during construction
- WSDOT / interstate design and safety requirements

## 4.2 Surface public realm
- park and landscape construction
- plazas, paths, stairs, ramps, and bike facilities
- lighting, furniture, play, and recreation
- planting, irrigation, stormwater features, and noise-buffering landscape treatments

## 4.3 Development-enabling infrastructure
- utility upgrades
- parcelization and access roads if any
- loading / service strategy
- podium, foundation, or structural capacity if development is integrated
- public-space improvements that make commercial frontage viable

## 4.4 Soft costs
- planning, environmental review, engineering, design, and permitting
- community engagement and owner’s representation
- legal and transaction costs for air-rights / ground-lease structures
- financing costs and contingency

## 4.5 Long-term operating and lifecycle costs
- landscape and public-space maintenance
- security / clean and safe / activation staff
- repair and replacement reserves
- structural inspection and lifecycle capital obligations

The last category matters. A lid that cannot be maintained will become a liability.

# 5. Capital funding strategy: likely sources

The most realistic capital stack is a **portfolio approach**. The project should assume from the beginning that different parts of the lid will be funded for different reasons.

## 5.1 Federal grants
These are likely essential for a project of this scale.

### Most plausible federal buckets
- **USDOT Reconnecting Communities / Neighborhood Access and Equity** style funding for barrier repair and neighborhood reconnection
- **RAISE** or similar multimodal / infrastructure grants
- potentially environmental justice, climate, or public-health oriented federal funds where applicable
- transportation / active transportation / safety programs depending on project scope

### Why the project is competitive
Lid I‑5 North can credibly frame itself as:
- freeway barrier repair
- multimodal safety and connectivity improvement
- environmental justice / public-health intervention
- open-space creation in an urban corridor
- economic reconnection between neighborhoods

## 5.2 State funding
State participation will likely be necessary because the project sits on the interstate system and implicates WSDOT.

Potential state roles include:
- transportation appropriations
- climate / clean transportation / active transportation programs
- capital support linked to mitigation, public access, or urban mobility
- direct legislative appropriation if the project gains political support

## 5.3 City and regional funding
Possible city / regional contributions include:
- Seattle transportation and parks capital funding
- city bonding backed by general revenues or dedicated local sources
- regional transportation or mobility partnerships
- targeted utility or green-infrastructure participation if project elements align

## 5.4 Philanthropy and civic fundraising
This is especially relevant if the project includes a significant park, cultural, or family-oriented public-space component.

Philanthropy is more plausible for:
- signature park and play features
- cultural / educational / art components
- community facilities
- public programming endowment
- environmental education or youth-oriented amenities

Presidio Tunnel Tops is the strongest precedent for a large philanthropic role in a highway-cap public-space project, though the institutional context there is unusual.

## 5.5 Private participation through development value
Private participation is most plausible where the project creates development parcels, air rights, or long-term lease opportunities.

This can take several forms:
- developer payment for air-rights parcels
- long-term ground lease revenue
- public-private partnerships in which private development helps finance portions of the lid
- negotiated contributions tied to added entitlement or site control

# 6. Land value capture and development-based funding tools

Washington does not offer a perfect freeway-lid funding tool, and this is where realism matters. The state does **not** have the same broad, flexible tax increment environment available in some other states. So the project should focus on tools that are actually usable in Washington.

## 6.1 The strongest likely value-capture tools for Lid I‑5 North

### A. Ground lease / air-rights development
This is likely one of the strongest tools available. If the public or a project entity controls buildable parcels created by the lid, it can retain ownership and lease them long-term, generating:
- upfront lease value
- recurring lease income
- control over tenant mix, affordability requirements, and design quality

### B. Targeted Local Improvement District (LID) or similar special assessment
Seattle and Washington have real precedent for benefit-based assessments. A targeted district could potentially capture some of the value created for nearby properties if a legally defensible benefit nexus can be shown.

This tool should be treated carefully:
- politically sensitive
- technically demanding
- requires a strong benefit case and district design
- probably more useful as one piece of the stack than as the core funding source

### C. Incentive zoning / negotiated public benefits
If adjacent rezoning or added development capacity is part of the project strategy, Seattle can potentially capture value through negotiated public benefits, affordable housing contributions, or other exactions tied to increased entitlement.

### D. Limited tax-increment style tools
Washington’s available tax-increment mechanisms are narrower than in some states. They may still play a supplementary role, but they are unlikely to be the sole backbone of the funding strategy.

## 6.2 Why the development component matters
The working assumption of **~6 buildable acres** is important because it gives the project a real value engine. Even if the project remains primarily a park and reconnection project, development value can help fund:
- portions of capital cost
- public-space improvements
- commercial buildout
- maintenance endowment or operating reserves

## 6.3 What not to assume
The project should **not** assume that value capture alone will fund the lid. That is too optimistic and politically risky. The better approach is:
> **use development value to reduce the public funding gap, not to replace the public case for the project.**

# 7. Development program possibilities

The 6 buildable acres should be treated strategically, not simply as leftover real estate.

## 7.1 Plausible development categories
- housing, including mixed-income or affordable housing components
- office / institutional / research / innovation space if market conditions support it
- neighborhood-serving retail at the ground floor
- community facilities, childcare, education, or cultural space
- hybrid mixed-use buildings with active commercial edges facing key lid public spaces

## 7.2 Why mixed-use matters
A mixed-use program can improve the project in three ways:
1. **financially** — by creating lease or sale value
2. **socially** — by putting more people and uses on the lid
3. **operationally** — by giving the public space daily users who support nearby businesses

## 7.3 Development should support the reconnection mission
The development parcels should not behave like isolated podium projects that happen to sit on a cap. They should reinforce the lid’s role as a seam between neighborhoods.

# 8. Case-study funding and business-case lessons

## 8.1 SR‑520 Montlake Lid
Montlake is relevant as a Washington precedent for a state-led transportation project with lid/open-space elements, but it is **not** a close economic analog for Lid I‑5 North. Its value is mostly in:
- demonstrating that freeway lid construction is institutionally possible in Washington
- showing the importance of integrating community expectations, open space, and transportation mitigation
- illustrating the degree to which state transportation projects can absorb lid-like elements when politically required

Lesson for Lid I‑5 North: **state partnership matters, but the urban business case here is stronger and more development-oriented than Montlake’s.**

## 8.2 Klyde Warren Park, Dallas
Klyde Warren is the classic example of a freeway cap that became a high-profile urban park and economic catalyst. Its lessons are powerful but must be used carefully:
- major philanthropic and private fundraising can be decisive
- signature programming and management matter as much as the physical park
- adjacent property value gains can be substantial
- a successful lid can become a citywide destination rather than only a neighborhood amenity

Lesson for Lid I‑5 North: **operations, programming, and civic identity are part of the economic model, not afterthoughts.**

## 8.3 Presidio Tunnel Tops, San Francisco
Presidio Tunnel Tops shows the power of a public-space-first lid / cap project funded through a blend of public and philanthropic support, with extensive community co-design and a strong conservancy-like operating context.

Lesson for Lid I‑5 North: **a lid with a compelling public narrative and high-quality public realm can attract philanthropic support, especially if the park and civic-repair story is strong.**

## 8.4 Portland Rose Quarter highway cap / reconnecting communities work
Portland is relevant because it shows how a highway cap can be framed through racial justice, neighborhood repair, and redevelopment of a freeway-damaged district. It also demonstrates the complexity of implementation, cost escalation, and agency coordination.

Lesson for Lid I‑5 North: **the civic-repair narrative is powerful, but engineering, governance, and capital-stack complexity should not be underestimated.**

## 8.5 Othello station-area development
Othello is not a freeway lid, but it matters because it shows that transit-adjacent development does not automatically create the business district a community wants. Commercial stabilization, merchant support, and affordable space matter.

Lesson for Lid I‑5 North: **if the project wants neighborhood-serving commerce rather than generic retail, it must design for it.**

# 9. Business development and commercial activation strategy

## Why this belongs in the economics document
Lid I‑5 North is not only a parks, mobility, or civic-repair project. It is also a **business-development opportunity**. The corridor between the **University District** and **Wallingford** contains two strong neighborhood economies, yet the freeway edge around **NE 45th / I‑5** and **NE 50th / I‑5** is commercially weak. The result is a missed economic seam: there is substantial nearby demand, but little reason for that demand to stop, spend, or linger at the freeway crossing itself.

The Seattle station-area case studies suggest a clear conclusion:

> **The strongest economic opportunity of Lid I‑5 North is to turn the freeway edge from a business dead zone into a connective commercial seam between two already-successful districts.**

That is a better starting point than many transit-oriented development sites. The project does not need to manufacture demand from nothing. It needs to **capture, slow, and organize demand that is already nearby but currently suppressed by the freeway barrier**.

---

# 10. What the Seattle case studies imply for business development

## Othello: local business ecosystems need deliberate support
Othello shows that a project can bring transit, housing, and new development without automatically producing the neighborhood business district people want. Seattle had to supplement rezoning and TOD with **retail analysis, merchant outreach, and commercial-stability work**. The lesson is that if Lid I‑5 North wants local-serving businesses rather than generic high-rent retail, it must actively design for them.

## Mount Baker: access and zoning do not automatically create a center
Mount Baker shows that even a major transit node can struggle commercially if the surrounding environment is fragmented, traffic-dominated, confusing, or unpleasant on foot. The lesson is that Lid I‑5 North’s public realm—its crossings, sight lines, edges, and comfort—will directly shape whether businesses succeed.

## Columbia City: human-scale commercial fabric and stewardship matter
Columbia City suggests that business districts are stronger when they have:
- a legible center,
- small storefronts,
- a recognizable identity,
- and some form of ongoing district stewardship.

Lid I‑5 North will not inherit Columbia City’s historic main street, but it can adopt the same structural logic: **human-scale commercial grain + district management + a clear identity.**

# 11. The economic opportunity unique to Lid I‑5 North

## 11.1 The project sits between two thriving business districts
This is the crucial difference between Lid I‑5 North and the Rainier Valley station areas.

The lid corridor lies between:
- the **University District**, with strong retail, food, nightlife, student demand, and regional transit access; and
- **Wallingford**, with a stable residential base, neighborhood retail, and a strong local-serving commercial identity.

The problem is not lack of nearby economic activity.  
The problem is that the freeway suppresses the relationship between those two districts.

## 11.2 Today the freeway edge behaves like a commercial void
The areas nearest **NE 45th / I‑5** and **NE 50th / I‑5** are poor business environments because they are dominated by:
- noise and visual blight from the freeway,
- difficult or unpleasant pedestrian crossings,
- awkward edge conditions and leftover transportation land,
- little reason to pause or linger,
- and no coherent district identity at the seam.

## 11.3 A lid can change the economics of the edge
A lid creates business opportunity in three ways:

### A. It removes the “dead zone” effect of the freeway edge
Instead of walking past retaining walls, ramps, and noisy edges, people can move through parkland, plazas, paths, and active frontage.

### B. It creates new frontage and new addresses
A lid can create corners, edges, and pathways where small commercial spaces could plausibly succeed.

### C. It captures movement that already exists
Students, residents, workers, and visitors already move between the U‑District and Wallingford. The lid can create places where that movement turns into economic activity.

This is the central commercial-development thesis of the project.

---

# 12. Commercial strategy: build a seam, not a freestanding mall

The right model for Lid I‑5 North is **not** to create a self-contained shopping district. It is to create a **stitched commercial seam** between two existing neighborhood economies.

That implies three strategic principles.

## Principle 1: Use the lid to create places to stop, not just paths to cross
A purely connective lid may improve mobility while generating limited business value. To support commerce, the lid needs places where people:
- sit,
- eat,
- watch,
- meet,
- study,
- attend events,
- or bring children and friends.

## Principle 2: Focus on human-scale neighborhood commerce
The project should not assume that the right outcome is a line of large-format retail bays. A more resilient model is:
- small storefronts,
- micro-retail / kiosk spaces,
- one or two anchors,
- and public programming that feeds nearby businesses.

## Principle 3: Treat the 45th and 50th crossings as different business opportunities
The commercial role of **NE 45th** should not be identical to that of **NE 50th**.

---

# 13. Recommended commercial structure for Lid I‑5 North

## 13.1 NE 45th Gateway Node
NE 45th is the stronger candidate for a more active commercial node because it sits closer to:
- the University District commercial core,
- major transit movement,
- student foot traffic,
- and the broader Brooklyn / University Way ecosystem.

### Recommended commercial profile
- coffee / bakery / breakfast-lunch uses
- student-oriented food and beverage
- study / cowork / tutoring / meeting space
- small grocery / provisions if feasible
- flexible event-supporting food and retail
- visible outdoor seating where noise and wind conditions can be managed

### Strategic role
This node should behave as the **higher-volume, more visible, U‑District-facing gateway** to the lid.

## 13.2 NE 50th Neighborhood Edge Node
NE 50th is better suited to a quieter, more Wallingford-facing, neighborhood-serving commercial edge.

### Recommended commercial profile
- café or bakery
- neighborhood restaurant / casual evening use
- childcare- or family-serving uses
- local services: wellness, clinic, repair, studio, boutique retail
- flexible community room or workshop space with a commercial edge

### Strategic role
This node should behave as the **calmer local edge** that draws Wallingford residents onto the lid and gives the park a daily neighborhood customer base.

## 13.3 Micro-retail and kiosk strategy
The project should plan from the outset for **low-cost, small-footprint commercial formats**, not only permanent storefronts.

### Possible formats
- food kiosks
- seasonal market stalls
- book / flower / gift kiosks
- bike repair / coffee carts
- incubator stalls for local entrepreneurs
- weekend market infrastructure

### Why this matters
Micro-retail lowers entry barriers, allows experimentation, and can activate the lid long before larger commercial buildout is complete.

## 13.4 One memorable destination use
The lid probably needs at least **one stronger draw** beyond passive open space.

This does not need to be a major tourist attraction. It could be:
- a pavilion café / beer garden
- a greenhouse or winter garden
- a family play feature with adjacent food uses
- an event terrace or small performance space
- a community room with active food frontage

The purpose is simple: create a reason to visit the lid intentionally, not only to pass through it.

# 14. How to maximize the chance of commercial success

## 14.1 Design commercial spaces for real neighborhood tenants
If the project wants local-serving and independent businesses, it needs to create spaces they can actually occupy.

### Design implications
- smaller bay widths
- shallow flexible spaces
- shared back-of-house strategies where possible
- utility and venting layouts that support food uses
- storefronts opening directly onto active public spaces
- careful noise and weather mitigation for outdoor seating

## 14.2 Treat affordable commercial space as a project objective
This is the main Othello lesson.

Possible tools include:
- reduced-rent spaces for local operators
- phased rent escalations
- nonprofit or mission-driven master leasing
- incubator stalls and temporary spaces
- tenant-improvement support
- public ownership or long-term ground lease structures that preserve some control over tenant mix

## 14.3 Build the public realm first, or at least simultaneously
Commercial success depends on the lid feeling safe, legible, and comfortable. That means:
- clear east–west walking routes
- strong sight lines
- lighting and visibility
- places to sit and gather
- noise buffering and landscape design
- active edges rather than blank walls or exposed service areas

## 14.4 Phase the commercial program
The project should not overbuild retail at the outset.

A more resilient sequence would be:
1. establish the core public spaces and crossings;
2. open one stronger 45th node and one smaller 50th node;
3. deploy micro-retail / market infrastructure early;
4. expand permanent commercial space as demand is proven.

## 14.5 Create a stewardship and operating model from the start
This is the Columbia City lesson.

Someone needs to be responsible for:
- tenant mix
- programming and events
- clean and safe operations
- marketing and district identity
- coordination between Wallingford, the U‑District, the City, and private or nonprofit partners

Without that function, the commercial strategy will be weaker.

# 15. Operating revenue and long-term maintenance

The project should plan for long-term operations early, not after design.

## 15.1 Likely operating cost drivers
- landscape and park maintenance
- janitorial and clean-and-safe functions
- event staffing and programming
- lighting, utilities, and repairs
- structural inspections and lifecycle reserves
- district management / marketing / tenant coordination if commercial space is included

## 15.2 Possible operating revenue sources
- ground lease income from development parcels
- commercial rents
- event revenue / concessions / kiosk fees
- philanthropy or conservancy support for programming
- city operating support if the project is treated as a major public park / civic space
- assessment district or BIA-like mechanism if politically feasible

The project should consider a model where **some development and commercial revenue is intentionally dedicated to public-space operations**, rather than being treated only as capital recapture.

# 16. Funding scenarios

These are not formal pro formas. They are strategic scenarios for how the project might be structured.

## Scenario A: Public-space-first lid with limited development
### Characteristics
- heavy reliance on federal + state + city capital
- strong philanthropic campaign for park / civic components
- modest development parcels and modest commercial activation
- operating model closer to a conservancy-supported park

### Best if
- the political and civic narrative emphasizes reconnection, open space, and environmental/public-health benefits
- development appetite is limited
- structural or neighborhood constraints reduce buildable area

## Scenario B: Hybrid park + mixed-use district
### Characteristics
- substantial public funding still required
- meaningful use of buildable parcels and ground lease revenue
- targeted commercial activation at 45th and 50th
- possible affordable housing or community-serving development
- stronger long-term self-support through lease revenue and district management

### Best if
- the project wants both a major public realm and a durable economic engine
- there is enough buildable land and entitlement support to make development value meaningful

## Scenario C: Development-led lid with strong value-capture emphasis
### Characteristics
- more aggressive use of development parcels, air rights, and PPP structures
- public funding still needed for core lid infrastructure but reduced relative to total cost
- greater pressure for larger buildings, higher rents, and stronger market-rate program

### Best if
- market conditions are strong and politically acceptable
- the project is willing to tolerate more intensive development and a less park-dominant identity

### Caution
This is probably the riskiest scenario politically and programmatically. It may undermine the civic-repair narrative if pushed too far.

# 17. Recommended funding posture

For Lid I‑5 North, the most credible strategy is **Scenario B: a hybrid park + mixed-use district**.

That means:

## Capital stack
- federal reconnection / multimodal / EJ / infrastructure grants
- state transportation and climate support
- city / regional capital participation
- targeted philanthropy for park and civic components
- development value through ground lease / air rights / PPP where appropriate
- selective use of LID or other value-capture tools if politically and legally feasible

## Economic-development posture
- use the buildable acres as a value engine, but not the sole rationale
- prioritize commercial activation at the seam between Wallingford and the U‑District
- design for local-serving commerce and public life, not just generic retail

## Governance posture
- create an operating / stewardship structure early
- separate, if necessary, the roles of:
  - public-space owner/operator
  - development entity / ground lessor
  - district manager / commercial steward

# 19. Bottom line

Lid I‑5 North should not be framed as “a park that happens to have development” or “a real-estate play that happens to have a park.” It is both less cynical and more ambitious than that.

Its best economic case is this:

- it repairs a freeway wound between **Wallingford** and the **University District**;
- it creates public space and environmental value that are independently worth funding;
- it unlocks a limited but meaningful amount of development and commercial value;
- and it can turn the weak edges around **NE 45th / I‑5** and **NE 50th / I‑5** into a stitched seam of neighborhood commerce, public life, and daily pedestrian activity.

If the project gets the capital stack, public realm, commercial grain, and stewardship model right, it can become a **major civic-repair project with a credible long-term economic engine**.

If it gets those things wrong, it may still be a worthwhile lid—but it will leave much of its economic, social, and political potential unrealized.
