# PROJECT_HISTORY_AND_CONTEXT.md
## Lid I-5 North Project
### Historical, Cultural, and Urban Context

---

# Purpose

This document provides historical and community context for the Lid I-5 North project. It explains how the current freeway corridor came to exist, how Interstate 5 reshaped nearby neighborhoods, why reconnection has become an important public objective, and how the project fits within broader changes in urban planning and transportation.

The document is intended for project participants, elected officials, consultants, researchers, grant writers, interested citizens, and future collaborators.

---

# Executive Summary

Interstate 5 is one of the most important transportation corridors in the Pacific Northwest. It is also one of the most significant physical barriers within Seattle.

Built in the mid-twentieth century, I-5 improved regional mobility but permanently changed the physical and social structure of many neighborhoods, including Wallingford, the University District, Green Lake, and Roosevelt. These communities remain connected by bridges and crossings, but the freeway still functions as a major divide. It affects the environment, social connection, local movement, and business opportunity.

The **Lid I-5 North** concept comes from a growing recognition that urban transportation infrastructure should be evaluated by more than its ability to move vehicles over long distances at high speeds. It should also be judged by how well it moves people, how it affects public space, how it shapes environmental quality, and how it supports neighborhood cohesion.

The project is part of a broader effort to repair, reconnect, and reimagine urban spaces shaped by twentieth-century transportation decisions. Similar conversations are happening in other parts of Seattle, including downtown, South Park, and Roanoke.

# Before Interstate 5

## Indigenous Landscape

Before European settlement, the area was home to Coast Salish peoples, including the ancestors of the Duwamish and related communities. Travel routes followed topography, waterways, ridges, and natural gathering places.

I-5 is often described as a barrier. The Indigenous landscape was organized around connections. The areas now called Wallingford, the University District, Ravenna, and Green Lake were part of a highly interconnected network of trails, waterways, fishing grounds, and seasonal gathering places.

Long before the area was called Brooklyn or the University District, the Portage Bay shoreline was part of a Lushootseed-speaking Duwamish landscape that included wəq̓iq̓əb, "place of frogs," a name that reflects the wetland ecology of the bay and the deep Indigenous history of the corridor.

**Turning our focus away from the freeway toward community connection is an important mindset shift.**

---

## Early Seattle Development (*ca.* 1890s - 1940s)

During the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, Seattle's population grew from 80,671 in 1890 to 467,591 in 1950. Streetcar lines connected neighborhoods, and development followed those routes. The Seattle Municipal Railway connected downtown to the University District, and Wallingford had several lines, including routes along N 45th Street and Stone Way.

NE 45th Street became one of the most important east-west commercial streets north of downtown, with commercial clusters around Wallingford Avenue N, Stone Way N, Latona Avenue NE, and University Way NE. Residential districts expanded outward from downtown, especially in the University District, Latona, Fremont, and Wallingford.

Neighborhood identity grew around transportation corridors, business centers, schools, and institutions. Interlake Elementary, Lincoln High School, the Good Shepherd convent and home, the University Book Store, The Ave, and Wallingford's 45th Street business district all helped shape local civic life.

The district was built around transit and walkability. As streetcar suburbs, Wallingford and the University District were organized around local access rather than freeway-speed travel.

**I-5 cut through an already-established urban district rather than creating one.**

---

# The Rise of the Automobile (*ca.* 1940s - 1970s)

From roughly the 1940s through the 1960s, U.S. transportation planning became strongly automobile-centered. Federal, state, and local policy prioritized urban freeways, suburban road access, and traffic flow over transit, walking, and neighborhood continuity.

## National Context

After World War II, federal policy strongly favored automobile transportation. Streetcar and trolley lines were abandoned in favor of road construction and automobile travel.

The Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, signed during the Eisenhower administration, launched the Interstate Highway System. Cities across the United States were encouraged to build high-capacity urban freeways.

The goals were economic growth, national defense, regional mobility, and suburban expansion. Proponents argued that a continuous highway network would reduce travel times and freight costs, provide evacuation routes during the Cold War, make cross-state travel easier, and open outlying land for large residential development.

---

## Seattle's Participation

Seattle embraced freeway construction as part of a broader modernization effort.

Planning assumptions of the era emphasized traffic throughput, vehicle capacity, and regional accessibility. Success was measured largely by "level of service" metrics, which calculated how fast cars could move through a corridor. This emphasis led to plans for an extremely dense highway network. Beyond I-5, planners proposed the R.H. Thomson Expressway, a parallel 15-mile north-south highway about one mile east.

Planners also assumed that downtown Seattle would remain the region's dominant employment center, while King County's outlying areas would house much of the workforce. Highways were treated as the essential links between them.

Far less attention was paid to neighborhood cohesion, environmental impacts, pedestrian experience, and long-term social consequences. Construction of I-5 cut a massive open trench through Seattle and permanently separated Wallingford and Green Lake from the University District and Roosevelt, turning a walkable slope into an artificial canyon.

**Beginning in the late 1960s, this approach was increasingly contested.** By the 1970s, transportation planning began to reintroduce transit, environmental review, and broader metropolitan planning.

---

# Construction of Interstate 5

## Physical Transformation

Construction of I-5 required large-scale excavation, property acquisition, utility relocation, and street modifications that disrupted formerly continuous neighborhood grids. Freeway routes were often chosen through disadvantaged communities. Some wealthier communities were able to prevent or delay freeway construction, such as South Pasadena, California.

---

## Immediate Effects

The freeway immediately improved regional travel. With the opening of the Ship Canal Bridge, travel times between downtown Seattle and northern suburbs were reduced by 30 to 60 percent.

At the same time, I-5 produced the negative consequences described above.

**The resulting I-5 corridor created a linear trench through Seattle, effectively dividing the city in half.**

---

# Wallingford and the University District

The two neighborhoods at the center of this project are Wallingford and the University District. Instead of feeling like parts of a continuous cityscape, today they feel and function like separate communities.

Although the neighborhoods are physically separated by less than 100 yards, the freeway creates a substantial barrier between them. The few crossings that exist were designed primarily for automobiles.

---

## Existing Crossings

Current freeway crossings include NE 45th Street, NE 50th Street, NE 40th Street, NE 65th Street, and Ravenna Boulevard. These crossings provide passable routes between the east and west sides of the freeway trench, but they do not mitigate the psychological effects or physical-safety risks. In many cases, they intensify them.

At NE 45th and NE 50th, pedestrians and cyclists are limited to narrow sidewalks next to dense, sometimes high-speed traffic. A six-inch curb separates people from vehicles. Designated bicycle crossings exist at the Burke-Gilman Trail and Ravenna Boulevard, more than a mile apart. Noise levels can reach 80 dB, just below the 85 dB threshold associated with risk from prolonged exposure.

**Plans to add a protected bicycle crossing on NE 45th Street as late as 2022 were scrapped in favor of prioritizing automobile traffic.**

---

## Perceived and Real Separation of Neighborhoods

Many residents describe the freeway as a dividing line, a source of dead-end streets, and an unpleasant crossing experience. The result is reduced interaction between adjacent neighborhoods.

Commercial zoning on streets feeding the I-5 ramps, especially NE 45th and NE 50th, also changed. Traditional zero-lot-line storefronts with wide sidewalks gave way in places to drive-throughs, gas stations, strip malls, and surface parking lots. This change is especially apparent on NE 45th Street.

Social outcomes can differ sharply across the freeway. Crime, housing accessibility, and neighborhood conditions can vary greatly between Wallingford and the University District, even on streets that are physically close to one another.

**The I-5 freeway, as it exists today, creates divided communities.**

# The Freeway as Urban Infrastructure

## Benefits

Interstate 5 provides significant regional benefits that extend beyond Wallingford and the University District. It is a major north-south trucking route through Seattle, carrying an estimated 5.8 million semi-trucks and commercial vehicles annually. The Ship Canal Bridge carries approximately 240,000 vehicles daily in both directions. The freeway links workers to business centers, supports freight movement, and connects suburbs, downtown, and communities east of Seattle.

**The freeway is, and remains, essential infrastructure for the larger Seattle region.**

---

## Costs and Impacts

The same infrastructure also imposes costs.

Environmental impacts include noise above 80 dB, elevated ultrafine particles and NOx near roadways, and heat-island effects that can make freeway-adjacent areas 5 to 12 degrees Fahrenheit warmer than residential areas with tree canopy.

Social impacts include neighborhood fragmentation, reduced walkability, and reduced social interaction. Freeways do not directly cause crime, but they can weaken the everyday social interactions that help communities feel safe and connected.

Visual and economic impacts are also significant. Large-scale infrastructure dominates the landscape, restricts local movement, reduces the customer base for nearby businesses, and can contribute to disinvestment and lower property values near the freeway.

**We can retain the benefits of our freeway infrastructure while still reconnecting our communities.**

# Changing Planning Philosophy

## Mid-Century Thinking

Historical transportation planning often prioritized vehicle movement, engineering efficiency, and regional mobility. Traffic was treated as predictable and modeled through engineering formulas. Straight-line routing, minimized land acquisition costs, and movement between growing suburbs and downtown business districts were central concerns.

---

## Contemporary Thinking

Modern transportation planning has increasingly shifted from vehicle throughput toward people, access, health, and spatial equity.

Earlier planners often treated crashes as an expected cost of high-speed transportation systems. Modern planners increasingly view traffic deaths as preventable infrastructure failures. Contemporary planning also places greater emphasis on reconnecting neighborhoods, investing in communities that have historically borne a disproportionate share of pollution and crashes, and creating public space where infrastructure once divided communities.

A structural lid over an open freeway trench can create a new ground plane for parks, civic facilities, public squares, housing, and other community uses that are badly needed in local neighborhoods. It can also reduce noise and reconnect walking, biking, and transit routes.

The central question has shifted from:

**How many vehicles can move through a corridor?**

to:

**How can transportation infrastructure support and co-exist with thriving communities?**

---

# The Reconnection Movement

Across North America, cities have begun reassessing infrastructure decisions made during the twentieth century. This movement includes freeway removals, highway caps, boulevard conversions, and neighborhood reconnection projects.

The goals are consistent: restore community connections, improve quality of life, create public space, and support economic development.

**Roughly 257 reconnections projects have been awarded nationwide since FY2022.**

---

# Why Lid Projects Have Emerged

A lid or cap allows land above transportation infrastructure to serve community purposes. The space above a freeway can become a park, trail, public gathering place, development site, community facility, or housing opportunity. The transportation function remains below, while new civic and neighborhood functions occur above.

Early lid projects were often viewed primarily as engineering solutions. More recent projects are increasingly viewed as community-building investments, economic development tools, and public-space projects. In this sense, a lid is not only a way to cover infrastructure. It is a way to recover land, repair connections, and create places that serve the neighborhoods around them.

**Highway lids can remediate the harms of transportation projects while preserving or even enhancing regional transportation.**

---

# Seattle Context

Seattle already contains several examples of freeway-overbuild infrastructure, including Freeway Park, the Washington State Convention Center expansion, and portions of the SR 520 corridor. These projects demonstrate that covering transportation infrastructure is technically feasible and can produce meaningful public benefits.

Freeway Park remains one of the most important local precedents. It created a landmark public space and a successful physical connection across I-5. It also offers lessons in visibility, maintenance, and perceived safety. Lawrence Halprin's original design used dense plantings and massive concrete walls to isolate the park from the freeway below, but over time these features obscured sightlines and made the park harder to navigate. Its water features have also been expensive to maintain, and secluded paths contributed to a lasting perception of safety concerns.

**Our history with freeway lids can and should inform future design decisions.**

---

# Why Lid I-5 North Is Different

Unlike downtown projects, Lid I-5 North focuses on residential communities adjacent to the freeway. It also has the potential to leverage the surrounding landscape to reduce lid cost, improve daily life, strengthen commercial and transit access, create public space, improve walkability, and potentially support housing on adjacent property.

The project offers a unique opportunity to rethink how freeways interact with neighborhoods, how public space is created, and how infrastructure investments create community value.  

**Lid I-5 North focuses on restoring and reconnecting residential communities.**

---

# Community Identity

Wallingford and the University District have distinct identities, but they share important values. Wallingford is known for strong neighborhood identity, walkability, local businesses, and civic engagement. The University District is shaped by educational institutions, international heritage, transit access, and cultural activity.

Both communities value public space, walkability, safety, environmental quality, and local character.  

**Connection will enhance all adjacent communities**

---

# Our Historical Narrative

The project can be understood through three eras.

**Era 1: Connection (1890 - 1940).** Neighborhoods developed with strong local connections.

**Era 2: Division (1940 - present).** Construction of Interstate 5 introduced a major barrier that remains largely unmitigated today.

**Era 3: Reconnection (our future).** The present generation has an opportunity to reconnect neighborhoods while preserving regional mobility.

This narrative is historically grounded. It does not assign blame. It approaches the problem with curiosity and asks what can be repaired, improved, and learned.

---

# Historical Risks

We can learn from the successes and failures of similar projects.

*The freeway should not be portrayed solely as a mistake.* It provides significant regional economic and transportation benefits. The objective is not to recreate the past. The objective is to create a better future.

*Historical impacts should not be exaggerated.* Neighborhoods adapted in many ways, and the project should focus on measurable opportunities rather than symbolic claims alone.

# Working Conclusion

The Lid I-5 North project exists because transportation infrastructure shapes communities in ways that extend beyond mobility.

Interstate 5 successfully connected cities and regions across the Pacific Northwest, but it also changed the physical and social relationships between adjacent Seattle neighborhoods. Wallingford and the University District remain close on a map, yet the freeway has made them feel farther apart in daily life.

*That separation is not inevitable.* It is the result of choices made in another era, under a different set of planning assumptions. We now have the chance to make a better set of choices.

The question before us is not simply whether we can cover a freeway. The question is whether we are willing to use infrastructure investment to repair connections, create public space, improve environmental conditions, support local businesses, and build a healthier civic landscape for North Seattle.

Lid I-5 North should move forward as a serious community project. The next step is to bring residents, businesses, institutions, agencies, technical experts, and elected leaders into a shared process: document the harms, define the opportunities, test feasible designs, and build the public will needed to reconnect these neighborhoods.

**The freeway will continue to serve the region. The land above it can serve the community.**
