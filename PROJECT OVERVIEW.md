# Lid I-5 North Project Knowledge Base

## Executive Summary

The Lid I-5 North Project is a conceptual and planning effort focused on reconnecting neighborhoods currently divided by Interstate 5 in north-central Seattle, particularly the Wallingford and University District communities. The project explores the feasibility, benefits, costs, and community implications of constructing a structural lid over portions of I-5 and creating new public spaces, transportation connections, and development opportunities above the freeway.

The project is motivated by several objectives:

- Reconnect communities separated by freeway infrastructure.
- Improve pedestrian and bicycle connectivity.
- Create new public open space in a dense urban environment.
- Reduce visual and acoustic impacts associated with the freeway.
- Improve neighborhood identity and cohesion.
- Support long-term economic development.
- Explore environmental and public health benefits.

The project remains in an exploratory stage. Significant questions remain regarding engineering feasibility, funding, governance, public support, environmental impacts, and long-term maintenance obligations.

This document is intended to serve as an onboarding resource for project participants, researchers, consultants, community advisory board members, and future AI assistants.

# Project Description

## Background

Interstate 5 was constructed through Seattle during the mid-20th century and created a significant physical and psychological barrier between neighborhoods.

In the study area, I-5 separates:

- Wallingford
- University District
- Roosevelt (indirectly)
- Adjacent residential communities
- Regional bicycle and pedestrian corridors

The freeway provides regional mobility but imposes costs on adjacent neighborhoods including:

- Noise
- Air pollution
- Visual blight
- Reduced walkability
- Reduced neighborhood cohesion
- Safety concerns associated with underpasses and freeway edges

The Lid I-5 North concept investigates whether portions of the freeway corridor could be covered and repurposed for public benefit.

# Crime and Public Safety Considerations

## Existing Conditions

Previous discussions have identified a visible concentration of crime incidents near portions of the freeway corridor.

Observed patterns include:

- Freeway-edge effects
- Underpass activity concentrations
- Reduced natural surveillance
- Lower perceived safety

## Working Hypothesis

Improved urban design may reduce crime opportunity through:

- Increased pedestrian activity
- Better lighting
- Improved visibility
- Greater neighborhood integration
- Increased informal surveillance

## Supporting Examples

Projects frequently cited include:

- Klyde Warren Park (Dallas)
- Rose Kennedy Greenway (Boston)
- Freeway Park renovation (Seattle)

## Important Caveat

Crime reduction should not be assumed.

Research findings across cities are mixed.

Crime displacement effects should be examined.

Additional empirical analysis is required.
