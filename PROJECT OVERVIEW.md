# Lid I-5 North Project Overview

## Purpose

This document is a working overview of the Lid I-5 North project.

It is meant to give new collaborators a clear starting point: what the project is, why it matters, what problems it is trying to solve, what questions remain open, and how the work should move forward.

The deeper historical and cultural narrative is developed in `PROJECT_HISTORY_AND_CONTEXT.md`. This overview builds from that document, but it is more practical. It is the project frame: the argument, the opportunity, the risks, and the path toward a serious public process.

---

## Executive Summary

Interstate 5 is essential regional infrastructure. It moves people, freight, and economic activity through Seattle and across the Pacific Northwest. It is also one of the largest physical barriers in the city.

In North Seattle, I-5 separates Wallingford, the University District, Green Lake, Roosevelt, and nearby residential communities. These neighborhoods are close on a map, but the freeway makes them feel farther apart in daily life. People can cross at NE 45th, NE 50th, NE 40th, NE 65th, Ravenna Boulevard, and the Burke-Gilman Trail, but many of those crossings are loud, narrow, uncomfortable, and designed primarily around cars.

The Lid I-5 North project asks a direct question:

**Can we preserve the regional transportation function of I-5 while reconnecting the neighborhoods above it?**

The answer may be yes. A structural lid over parts of the freeway could create a new ground plane for parks, paths, civic space, community facilities, housing opportunities, and better east-west connections. It could reduce the daily impacts of noise and visual blight, improve walking and biking, support local businesses, and create public space in a part of Seattle where land is scarce and expensive.

This is not a proposal to erase the freeway or pretend it has no value. The freeway serves the region and will continue to do so. The point is different:

**The freeway can continue to serve the region. The land above it can serve the community.**

Lid I-5 North is still in an exploratory stage. Major questions remain about engineering, cost, governance, funding, design, equity, maintenance, public support, and phasing. Those questions are real, and they should be taken seriously. But they are not reasons to avoid the project. They are the work.

## The Core Idea

The project is about reconnection.

Before I-5, the area now known as Wallingford, the University District, Green Lake, Ravenna, and Roosevelt was part of a more continuous urban landscape. Long before Seattle's street grid, the area was part of an Indigenous landscape organized around waterways, topography, gathering places, and movement. Later, streetcar lines, neighborhood business districts, schools, churches, and institutions helped create a walkable urban district.

I-5 cut through that established place. It improved regional mobility, but it also created a canyon through the city.

The Lid I-5 North concept starts from the recognition that infrastructure should be judged by more than vehicle throughput. It should also be judged by how it affects public space, neighborhood life, environmental quality, health, safety, and local economic opportunity.

This project is not nostalgia. It is not an attempt to return to a pre-freeway past. It is a chance to make a better next version of the city.

---

## The Project Area

The primary focus is the I-5 corridor between Wallingford and the University District, with connections to Green Lake, Roosevelt, Ravenna, and nearby residential areas.

Important east-west crossings and reference points include:

- NE 45th Street
- NE 50th Street
- NE 40th Street
- NE 65th Street
- Ravenna Boulevard
- The Burke-Gilman Trail
- The University District light rail station area
- Wallingford's 45th Street commercial district
- The University of Washington and University District business core

The exact project boundaries are not fixed. They should be defined through technical analysis and community engagement. Different lid segments may have different costs, benefits, design constraints, and implementation timelines.

The corridor should be studied as a system rather than a single isolated deck. The crossings, ramps, sidewalks, bicycle routes, bus connections, business districts, open spaces, and adjacent parcels all matter.

## Why This Matters

### The freeway divides places that should be close

Wallingford and the University District are separated by less than 100 yards in some places, but I-5 makes that short distance feel much larger. The freeway edge creates noise, exhaust, visual disruption, dead-end streets, and uncomfortable crossings.

For pedestrians and cyclists, especially at NE 45th and NE 50th, the experience is often unpleasant. Narrow sidewalks, freeway ramp traffic, high noise levels, and limited protection make the crossing feel like a place to move through quickly rather than a normal part of the neighborhood.

### The current crossings were not designed around people

The existing bridges and underpasses provide access, but they do not create comfort, safety, or civic connection. They are transportation leftovers: functional enough to cross, but not designed as good public places.

That matters. If a route feels hostile, fewer people use it. If fewer people use it, neighborhoods interact less. Businesses lose potential foot traffic. Public life thins out. Places that are physically near each other begin to feel socially separate.

### North Seattle needs public space

Seattle is growing, and North Seattle is growing with it. The University District is adding housing, students, jobs, and transit-oriented development. Wallingford, Green Lake, Ravenna, and Roosevelt all face pressure from population growth, housing demand, and limited public land.

A lid could create public space in a corridor where new land is otherwise almost impossible to assemble. That land could support parks, active transportation, community gathering, play, trees, small civic uses, and possibly housing or mixed-use development where appropriate.

### Infrastructure can repair as well as divide

Twentieth-century infrastructure often divided neighborhoods in the name of regional mobility. Twenty-first-century infrastructure should be capable of doing more than moving vehicles. It should also create value for the communities that live beside it.

Lid I-5 North belongs in that shift.

## Historical Frame

The project can be understood through three eras.

**Connection: 1890 to 1940.**  
Neighborhoods developed around streetcars, local business districts, schools, walking routes, and civic institutions. Wallingford, the University District, Latona, Green Lake, Ravenna, and Roosevelt were distinct places, but they were part of a connected urban fabric.

**Division: 1940 to the present.**  
The automobile era changed how cities were planned. Federal and state transportation policy prioritized freeways, vehicle capacity, commuter movement, freight, and suburban growth. I-5 brought real regional benefits, but it also cut a trench through Seattle and permanently changed the relationship between adjacent neighborhoods.

**Reconnection: our future.**  
Seattle now has the chance to preserve the mobility value of I-5 while repairing some of the harm created by the open freeway trench. A lid is one way to do that. It is not the only tool, but it is one of the most direct and ambitious tools available.

This narrative does not require exaggeration. I-5 was not built for no reason, and it should not be described only as a mistake. The point is that cities inherit decisions from earlier generations, and each generation gets to decide what to repair, what to improve, and what to build next.

## Project Goals

### Reconnect neighborhoods

The first goal is to make it easier, safer, and more natural to move between Wallingford, the University District, Green Lake, Roosevelt, Ravenna, and surrounding communities.

That means better pedestrian crossings, better bicycle connections, better transit access, and a stronger sense that these places are part of the same city.

### Create public space

A lid could create parks, plazas, play areas, trails, gardens, viewpoints, and civic gathering spaces above land that is currently consumed by freeway trench.

This public space should not be treated as decoration. It should be useful, visible, accessible, and maintainable.

### Improve safety and comfort

The project should improve real and perceived safety. Better lighting, visibility, natural surveillance, active uses, and clearer routes can all help.

Crime reduction should not be assumed or oversold. The better claim is that good urban design can reduce opportunities for disorder and make public spaces feel more welcoming and legitimate.

### Support walking, biking, and transit

The lid should strengthen the everyday mobility network. It should help people move between homes, businesses, schools, transit, parks, and regional trails without being forced into uncomfortable freeway-edge environments.

NE 45th and NE 50th are especially important because they carry both local trips and freeway ramp traffic. Any serious project needs to examine how those crossings can work better for pedestrians, cyclists, transit riders, drivers, freight, and emergency access.

### Reduce environmental impacts

A lid may reduce noise and visual exposure from the freeway. It may also create opportunities for tree canopy, planting, stormwater management, habitat, shade, and climate resilience.

Air quality benefits need careful study. Covering a freeway can change how pollutants move through the corridor and may require ventilation or other mitigation. The project should be honest about what is known, what is likely, and what requires technical analysis.

### Strengthen local business districts

Better east-west movement could increase foot traffic between neighborhoods and support businesses on both sides of I-5. Wallingford's 45th Street district, The Ave, and nearby commercial areas could all benefit from a more comfortable public realm and stronger connections.

### Build civic identity

Lid I-5 North should be more than an engineering project. Done well, it could become a civic landmark: a visible statement that Seattle can invest in repair, public space, and neighborhood connection while still supporting regional mobility.

## What a Lid Could Include

The final program should come from technical analysis and community engagement, but possible uses include:

- Public parks and green space
- Walking and biking routes
- Safer and more comfortable crossings
- Plazas and flexible civic gathering areas
- Playgrounds or recreation space
- Tree canopy and planting
- Stormwater features
- Community facilities
- Public art and cultural interpretation
- Small-scale retail or kiosks where appropriate
- Housing or mixed-use development on adjacent or structurally feasible sites

The project should avoid designing a lid as leftover space. The question is not simply how to cover the freeway. The question is what kind of place should exist above it.

## Design Principles

### Start with connection

Every design decision should be tested against the basic question: does this help people move between neighborhoods more safely, comfortably, and naturally?

### Make it visible and legible

Seattle already has lessons from Freeway Park. It is an important precedent, but it also shows the risks of hidden spaces, confusing routes, poor visibility, and high maintenance features.

Lid I-5 North should be easy to find, easy to navigate, and easy to understand.

### Design for daily use

The lid should serve everyday neighborhood life, not only special events or renderings. It should work for students, families, older adults, commuters, small businesses, cyclists, transit riders, dog walkers, and people simply trying to cross the freeway without feeling like they are crossing a freeway.

### Be honest about maintenance

Maintenance is not a detail to solve later. It is part of the design. Planting, lighting, materials, programming, drainage, security, and public restrooms all create long-term obligations. The project should be designed around what can be maintained well.

### Preserve regional mobility

The project should not begin from the assumption that I-5 disappears. I-5 remains a major regional corridor for freight, commuting, emergency travel, and economic activity. The lid concept is powerful precisely because it can add community value above the freeway while preserving transportation function below.

### Plan for equity from the beginning

Public investment can create benefits and pressures at the same time. Improved amenities may increase land values, raise rents, and accelerate displacement pressures. Equity cannot be an afterthought. The project needs early attention to housing affordability, business retention, access, governance, and who benefits from the public investment.

## Risks and Open Questions

The project should be ambitious, but it should also be clear-eyed.

### Cost

Freeway lids are expensive. Costs can include structure, ventilation, fire and life safety systems, seismic design, utilities, ramps, drainage, public space construction, operations, and long-term maintenance.

The project needs early cost ranges by segment, not one vague total number.

### Engineering feasibility

Different parts of the corridor will have different structural conditions. Width, depth, grades, ramp geometry, bridge locations, utilities, ventilation, seismic requirements, and WSDOT operations all matter.

Some segments may be feasible sooner than others.

### Construction disruption

Construction could affect traffic, businesses, residents, transit, freight, noise, dust, and access for multiple years. The project needs a realistic construction phasing strategy and a serious mitigation plan.

### Equity and displacement

A successful lid could increase nearby property values. That may be good for some owners and difficult for renters, small businesses, students, and lower-income residents.

The project should study anti-displacement strategies, affordable housing opportunities, business retention, and public ownership or stewardship models.

### Governance

Who owns the lid? Who maintains it? Who programs it? Who pays for operations? Who is accountable when something breaks?

These questions need answers before the project moves from vision to implementation.

### Public expectations

A lid can do a lot, but it cannot solve every neighborhood problem. It will not eliminate all freeway impacts. It may not deliver all desired uses in every location. The project should communicate clearly about tradeoffs and constraints.

### Air quality

Noise reduction is easier to explain than air quality improvement. Covering a freeway can reduce direct exposure in some locations but may concentrate emissions near portals or ventilation points. This requires careful technical study.

### Opportunity cost

Large public investments should be compared with other possible investments. The case for Lid I-5 North should explain why this project is worth pursuing, what benefits it provides that smaller interventions cannot, and how it can be phased intelligently.

- Identifying the most feasible early lid opportunities

- Building a coalition across neighborhoods and institutions
- Preparing for state and federal funding opportunities

## Case Studies and Lessons

Other projects can inform Lid I-5 North, but none should be copied directly.

### Freeway Park, Seattle

Freeway Park proves that Seattle can build public space over I-5. It also shows the importance of visibility, access, maintenance, programming, and perceived safety. The lesson is not simply "build a park over a freeway." The lesson is to design a place that is active, legible, maintainable, and connected to its surroundings.

### Klyde Warren Park, Dallas

Klyde Warren Park shows how a freeway lid can become a major civic space and economic development catalyst. It also shows the importance of programming, philanthropy, operations, and a strong public identity.

### Rose Kennedy Greenway, Boston

The Greenway shows the long-term redevelopment potential of reclaiming transportation corridors. It also demonstrates that governance, maintenance, and programming are as important as the initial construction.

### Other references

Additional projects worth studying include the Cap at Union Station in Columbus, the Deck Park Tunnel in Phoenix, the Stitch in Atlanta, SR 520 lid projects in the Seattle region, and reconnecting-community projects funded through recent federal programs.

## Community Engagement Approach

The engagement process should be open, practical, and early.

The project needs to ask:

- What crossings feel unsafe or uncomfortable today?
- Where do people actually want to walk and bike?
- What public spaces are missing?
- What benefits matter most to nearby residents and businesses?
- What concerns do people have about cost, construction, safety, and displacement?
- What would make the project feel worth it?
- What should not happen?

Different audiences will bring different priorities.

Residents may focus on safety, noise, access, housing, and neighborhood character. Businesses may focus on customer access, construction impacts, and visibility. Students may focus on walking, biking, transit, and affordability. Agencies may focus on operations, cost, safety standards, and maintenance. Elected officials may focus on public value, funding, equity, and delivery risk.

All of those perspectives belong in the process.

## The Story We Should Tell

The strongest story is simple:

Seattle built I-5 to connect the region. In doing so, it divided neighborhoods. Now we have a chance to keep the regional connection and repair the local one.

This story works because it is honest. It does not deny the value of the freeway. It does not overstate the past. It does not promise that a lid will fix everything. It says that public infrastructure has consequences, and public infrastructure can also be part of the repair.

Possible short framing:

**Lid I-5 North transforms a barrier into a connection, creating new public space while reconnecting neighborhoods that have been separated for decades.**

Even shorter:

**Keep the freeway. Reconnect the city.**

## Near-Term Work Plan

The next phase should turn the concept into a credible project.

Recommended next steps:

1. Define the study corridor and possible lid segments.
2. Map existing conditions, including crossings, ramps, sidewalks, bike routes, transit access, noise, air quality, heat, land use, crash history, crime patterns, and public space gaps.
3. Identify early technical constraints, including structure, geometry, ventilation, seismic design, utilities, and WSDOT operational requirements.
4. Develop preliminary lid scenarios with rough cost ranges.
5. Estimate potential benefits, including connectivity, public space, economic value, environmental improvement, and safety.
6. Build a coalition of residents, businesses, agencies, institutions, and elected officials.
7. Create a funding strategy tied to federal, state, local, philanthropic, and value-capture opportunities.
8. Begin community engagement before design assumptions harden.
9. Compare lid segments with smaller near-term improvements, including better crossings, protected bike facilities, lighting, sidewalks, and public realm upgrades.
10. Prepare a clear feasibility and public-value case.

## Working Conclusion

Lid I-5 North is a serious community project because the problem is real and the opportunity is rare.

I-5 will remain one of the most important transportation corridors in the region. But the neighborhoods beside it should not have to accept the open trench as a permanent limit on public space, walkability, safety, and civic life.

The project asks Seattle to think about infrastructure in a fuller way. Not just how fast vehicles move, but how people live around the systems we build. Not just what the freeway does for the region, but what the land above it could do for the community.

The next step is not to pretend all the answers are known. The next step is to organize the work: document the existing conditions, test the feasible segments, study the costs and benefits, listen to the people who use the corridor, and build the public will needed to reconnect these neighborhoods.

The freeway connected the region.  
Now we can reconnect the neighborhoods.
